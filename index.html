Dhamma Corner

In the current landscape of global volatility, leaders often find themselves caught in the cycle of reaction—responding to threats with threats, and to instability with more of the same. The early Buddhist teachings offer a profound alternative to this reactive state: the cultivation of upekkhā, or equanimity.

Equanimity is not indifference. It is the ability to maintain a steady heart in the midst of the eight worldly winds: gain and loss, fame and disrepute, praise and blame, pleasure and pain. When we look at the geopolitical maneuvering and the pressures on global supply chains, it is easy to be swept away by these winds. Yet, the Buddha taught that the one who remains unmoved is the one who understands the nature of conditioned things—that they are impermanent, subject to change, and ultimately beyond our absolute control.

For the decision-maker or the innovator, this is a call to strategic clarity. By recognizing the impermanent nature of these crises, one can act with precision rather than panic. When you are no longer driven by the fear of loss or the grasping for gain, your decisions become aligned with the long-term reality rather than the short-term noise. This is not to suggest passivity, but rather a profound form of active, clear-sighted engagement.

The practice is to observe the impulse to react—the anger at a geopolitical provocation, the anxiety over a market shift—and to see it as a conditioned phenomenon. By pausing, breathing, and grounding oneself in the present, one creates the space for wisdom to arise. This wisdom sees beyond the immediate conflict and recognizes the interconnectedness of all systems, allowing for solutions that are not just reactive, but transformative.

Ref: Lokavipatti Sutta: The Eight Worldly Conditions (AN 8.6)
