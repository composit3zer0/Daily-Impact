Dhamma Corner

In a world defined by the rapid delegation of decision-making to autonomous agents—the 'agentic economy'—the early Buddhist teachings offer a vital framework for maintaining clarity. We are increasingly tempted to view these systems as external entities that can solve our problems, yet the Buddha repeatedly emphasized that the capacity for discernment (yoniso manasikara) cannot be outsourced.

When we interact with complex systems, we often fall into the trap of 'view'—clinging to the idea that a particular technological solution will bring lasting security. However, the teaching on the Five Aggregates reminds us that all phenomena, including the digital landscapes we inhabit, are subject to change and lack inherent substance. The 'agent' in the machine is as conditioned and impermanent as the mind that created it.

For the leader or innovator, the practice is not to reject the tool, but to develop the 'faculty of wisdom' (panna) so that one is not enslaved by the tool's output. To act with integrity in an era of automation, one must cultivate a mind that is not swayed by the speed of the machine, but is anchored in the ethical awareness of the consequences of one's actions. As noted in the Kaccanagotta Sutta, the middle way involves avoiding the extremes of believing in a permanent 'self' that controls everything and the nihilistic belief that because everything is conditioned, nothing matters. We act, we innovate, we build—but we do so with the full awareness that these are conditioned processes, and our true agency lies in the quality of our intention.

Reference: Kaccānagotta Sutta (SN 12.15)
