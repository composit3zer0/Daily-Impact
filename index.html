Dhamma Corner

In our current climate of rapid technological and geopolitical shifts, the mind often seeks refuge in the illusion of control. We build complex infrastructure, negotiate treaties, and refine algorithms, believing that if we just optimize enough, we can secure our future against the winds of change. Yet, the Dhamma teaches us that this search for lasting security in external conditions is fundamentally flawed because it ignores the nature of anicca, or impermanence.

The IBM study regarding AI dependency highlights a profound irony: in our attempt to gain efficiency through AI, we have entangled ourselves in new webs of dependency. We have traded flexibility for speed, and now we find ourselves bound by the very tools designed to liberate us. This is a modern manifestation of upadana, or clinging. We cling to the vendor, the model, the infrastructure, believing these are the sources of our stability.

The practitioner, whether an innovator or a leader, is invited to cultivate sati, or mindfulness, not merely as a tool for focus, but as a mechanism for insight. When we observe the rise and fall of markets—or the rise and fall of our own internal ambitions—we see that they are subject to the same laws of arising and passing away. The peace deal in the Middle East, the fluctuations of oil, the rapid rise of SpaceX—all are transient phenomena. They are not "problems" to be solved once and for all, but processes to be understood.

True intelligence in an era of volatility is not the ability to control the outcome, but the capacity to remain steady amidst the change. By recognizing the limitations of our dependencies, we can act with greater clarity and detachment. We do not stop building or innovating; rather, we build with the understanding that these systems, too, are impermanent. We work with them, but we do not become them. In this way, we find a stable center that no market movement or geopolitical shift can disturb.

Personal Reflection

Today’s news is filled with stories of "lock-in"—systems so deeply integrated that you cannot escape them, and geopolitical deals that attempt to force order onto a chaotic world. It is easy to feel that your own autonomy is shrinking, squeezed between technological dependencies and the grand movements of global power. But consider the moments when you feel most "locked in" to a decision or a path. Is that constraint truly external, or is it a story you are telling yourself to avoid the discomfort of starting over?

What is one "essential" system in your life that you are afraid to let go of, and what would happen if you simply walked away?
