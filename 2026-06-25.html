Dhamma Corner

In a world where leaders are obsessed with "ghost downsizing" and the rapid deployment of autonomous agents, there is a pervasive sense of anxiety—a fear of being rendered obsolete. We witness the frantic attempt to hoard resources, secure supply chains, and define national interests through strategic maneuvering. Yet, the early Buddhist teachings offer a different perspective on the nature of these concerns.

The Buddha frequently pointed to the instability of conditioned things. Whether it is a corporate role, a national position, or a technological advantage, these states are subject to change. The suffering we experience—what the Pali texts call dukkha—arises precisely from our desperate attempt to find permanent security in a world characterized by impermanence (anicca). When we identify ourselves solely with our professional output or our geopolitical standing, we become fragile.

For the leader or innovator, the challenge is not to stop the progress of technology, nor to abandon the necessity of planning, but to cultivate a mind that is not "tangled." In the Sabba Sutta, the Buddha advises that one should abandon the tendency to view the world through the lens of "I" and "mine." When we look at a workforce reduction or a geopolitical crisis, the panic stems from the belief that "my" project, "my" company, or "my" country is the ultimate reality. By seeing these events as conditioned phenomena—arising and passing due to a complex web of causes and conditions—we gain the space to act with clarity and compassion rather than reactive fear.

True resilience is not found in building higher walls or faster algorithms, but in the radical acceptance of the changing nature of the world. By letting go of the need for absolute control, we become more capable of navigating the currents of change. We act because it is the right thing to do, not because we are driven by the fear of obsolescence.

Source: SN 35.23: The All (Sabba Sutta)
Personal Reflection

Today’s news is filled with the language of efficiency, downsizing, and strategic positioning. It is easy to view your own life through these same metrics—measuring your value by your productivity or your ability to stay ahead of the curve. But when you strip away the titles, the projects, and the "ghost" of what you think you should be, what remains? You are not the role you play in the market. You are the awareness that observes the change. When you stop trying to secure a future that is inherently uncertain, you might find that you have more room to actually be present.
