Dhamma Corner

In a world defined by strategic chokepoints and the rapid, often volatile, evolution of artificial intelligence, we are prone to forgetting the nature of the conditioned world. We invest our identity in the stability of trade routes, the sanctity of institutional law, and the promise of technological advancement. Yet, as the currents of change—be they geopolitical or meteorological—demonstrate, these structures are inherently unstable.

The Buddha taught that all conditioned things are subject to change and instability. When we anchor our sense of security in external arrangements, we become slaves to the shifting tides. The leader, the investor, the innovator—all are subject to the same law of impermanence (anicca). To cling to a particular outcome, a specific market index, or a fixed political order is to create the very conditions for suffering when those structures inevitably shift.

True intelligence, in the Dhamma sense, is not merely the ability to process data or navigate complex systems; it is the capacity to see clearly that which is changing. When one realizes that the "chokepoints" of our lives—our careers, our reputations, our power—are fluid, the grip of fear begins to loosen. We do not stop engaging with the world; rather, we engage without the desperation that comes from the illusion of permanence.

The path is not one of withdrawal, but of skillful presence. As you navigate the complexities of your day, observe the impulse to control, the anxiety of the unknown, and the desire for certainty. Recognize these as mental formations arising and passing away. By cultivating this awareness, you become like the sage who stands amidst the storm, neither resisting the wind nor being swept away by it.
