Dhamma Corner

In periods of intense structural fragmentation, when geopolitical blocs clash, supply chains fracture, and energy bottlenecks threaten technological expansion, the human mind instinctively reacts with anxiety, grasping, and defensive hostility. We look for stability in external arrangements—in bilateral nuclear pacts, diplomatic summits in Manila, or regulatory firewalls—forgetting that outward conditions are inherently unstable by their very constitution.

The early Buddhist teachings direct our attention away from the impossible task of freezing an uncooperative world into permanent security, pointing instead toward the cultivation of an unshakeable inner refuge. In the Cula-Malunkya Sutta, the Buddha uses the powerful simile of a person struck by a poisoned arrow, who refuses treatment until they know the exact caste, height, and origin of the archer. In the same way, when leaders and innovators are struck by market volatility or technological disruption, we waste precious mental energy investigating every symptom of external crisis while neglecting the immediate healing of our own reactivity.

True strategic clarity does not arise from frantic reactivity or defensive armor. It emerges from steady, unencumbered mindfulness that observes pleasant and unpleasant conditions with equanimity. When you see that economic shifts, power struggles, and digital transformations are subject to the universal law of impermanence, you stop fighting reality and begin working skillfully within it. By letting go of the internal craving for absolute control, decision-makers uncover a profound, quiet resilience that remains steady amidst the raging storms of global change.

Reference: Cūḷamālunkyovāda Sutta (MN 63)
