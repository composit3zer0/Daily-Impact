Dhamma Corner

In a world defined by the rapid oscillation between technological escalation and geopolitical blockade, the mind finds itself in a state of constant, reactive turbulence. We see this today in the tension surrounding the Strait of Hormuz and the frantic race to regulate—or pause—the expansion of artificial intelligence. These are not merely external events; they are the externalization of a mind that lacks a center.

The early Buddhist teachings remind us that suffering arises from craving and aversion—the desperate desire for things to be a certain way, and the equally desperate desire for them not to be. We crave the "sovereign" safety of our own systems and harbor aversion toward the unpredictable variables of the global order. Leaders and innovators today often fall into the trap of believing that by controlling the external environment—whether through military force or regulatory frameworks—they can secure the internal peace they seek. Yet, the Dhamma teaches that the world is inherently unstable, a process of arising and passing away (anicca).

To navigate this, one must cultivate the skill of "non-attachment" to the outcomes of these grand designs. This does not mean inaction or indifference. On the contrary, it means acting with full awareness, seeing the interdependent nature of these crises without being consumed by the anxiety they produce. When the Buddha speaks of the "unshakable heart," he refers to a mind that is no longer buffeted by the winds of gain and loss, praise and blame, fame and disrepute. For the decision-maker, this is the ultimate strategic advantage: the ability to observe the shifting currents of geopolitics and technology with clarity, choosing the path that serves the common good rather than the path dictated by fear.

By understanding that all phenomena—nations, technologies, and markets—are empty of inherent, permanent existence, you gain the freedom to innovate without the desperation of clinging. You act because it is the right thing to do, not because you are terrified of what happens if you do not.

Source: SN 35.88: The Simile of the Snake — Understanding that all things are subject to change and that clinging to them as "mine" leads to suffering.
