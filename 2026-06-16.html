Dhamma Corner

In today's headlines, we observe the world oscillating between the relief of a de-escalated conflict and the anxiety of rising climate threats and technological fragmentation. For the leader or innovator, this creates a constant state of flux. Early Buddhist teaching offers a profound lens for this: the recognition of anicca, or impermanence.

When we read of a 'tentative deal' in the Strait of Hormuz or a 'record high' in the stock market, we are seeing the world in its natural, shifting state. The suffering—or dukkha—that arises in our professional lives often stems from the desire to make these shifting conditions permanent. We want the peace deal to be absolute; we want the market rally to be eternal. Yet, the Dhamma reminds us that to cling to a temporary state as if it were fixed is the root of our dissatisfaction.

The practitioner of clarity does not ignore the news, nor do they become paralyzed by it. Instead, they cultivate upekkhā, or equanimity. This is not indifference. It is the ability to engage fully with the complexities of global strategy and technological change while maintaining an inner balance that is not shaken by the inevitable rise and fall of events. By acknowledging that the current order—whether political, economic, or environmental—is subject to change, the decision-maker can act with greater agility and less emotional friction.

As noted in the teachings, the wise person understands that whatever is subject to arising is also subject to ceasing. This insight allows for a more strategic, detached, and ultimately more effective approach to leadership. You are not the market; you are not the conflict; you are the awareness that observes the change.
