Dhamma Corner

In a world defined by the rapid, often volatile movement of capital, geopolitics, and technological disruption, the mind naturally seeks stability. We look to the markets, to policy, or to infrastructure, hoping to find a solid ground upon which to build our ambitions. Yet, as the headlines today remind us—from the shifting alliances in the Middle East to the sudden re-evaluations of scientific "truths"—the world is a domain of constant flux.

The early Buddhist teachings, particularly those found in the Pali Canon, emphasize the nature of anicca, or impermanence. When we invest our sense of identity or security in the external world, we are essentially building on shifting sands. The Buddha taught that suffering (dukkha) arises not from the change itself, but from our craving for things to remain a certain way—for peace to be permanent, for growth to be infinite, or for our own status to be unshakable.

For the decision-maker or the innovator, this is not a call to passivity, but a call to radical clarity. To act effectively in a complex environment, one must possess a mind that is not entangled with the outcome. When you observe the "risk-on" rallies or the sudden geopolitical shifts, see them for what they are: conditioned phenomena. By maintaining a steady, reflective awareness, you can navigate these waves without being swept away by them. The true leader is one who understands the nature of the terrain, moves with the flow of events, and remains anchored in an inner stillness that no external crisis can disturb.
