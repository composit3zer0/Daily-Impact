Dhamma Corner

In a world gripped by the volatility of markets, the friction of geopolitics, and the relentless speed of technological disruption, the mind is often pulled into a state of reactive agitation. We observe the rise and fall of indices, the shifting alliances of nations, and the breakthroughs of science, perceiving them as solid, objective realities that demand our constant, anxious attention.

The early Buddhist teachings remind us that this perception of "solid reality" is the root of our suffering. We look for stability in an inherently unstable environment. As the Buddha emphasized, all conditioned things are subject to the nature of arising and passing away. When we identify with the success of our portfolios, the stability of our supply chains, or the success of our technological ventures, we bind our inner peace to external conditions that are fundamentally beyond our control.

For the leader or innovator, this does not imply passivity. Rather, it invites a "wise engagement." When one recognizes the impermanent nature of these global shifts, one can act with clarity and purpose, free from the grasping that leads to distress. By cultivating the ability to witness these events without becoming entangled in the emotional fallout, one retains the capacity for decisive, thoughtful action. The goal is to act within the world while remaining unburdened by the world's inevitable fluctuations.

This is the practice of the Middle Way—engaging with the complexities of the modern era while maintaining the inner stillness that allows for true discernment. When you see the markets drop or the geopolitical landscape fracture, recognize it as the natural unfolding of conditions. By letting go of the need for these conditions to be other than what they are, you gain the freedom to navigate them with equanimity.

Source: AN 4.55: The Worldly Winds (Lokavipatti Sutta)
Personal Reflection

You have spent your day analyzing shifts in global power, the volatility of digital markets, and the rapid pace of technological progress. It is easy to view these events as a series of external problems to be solved or risks to be mitigated. Yet, pause for a moment to consider how much of your own identity is wrapped in the outcome of these events. If the market thrives, you feel capable; if it falters, you feel diminished. Your sense of "self" is often held hostage by the very impermanence you are trying to master.

*If you were to strip away the success of your ventures and the status of your decisions, what remains of you when the noise of the world finally settles?*
