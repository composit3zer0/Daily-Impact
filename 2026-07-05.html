Dhamma Corner

In the modern arena of high-stakes decision-making, we are often seduced by the allure of "control." We see this in the frantic attempts to regulate AI, the geopolitical maneuvers to secure mineral supply chains, and the desperate efforts to maintain market stability during times of transition. Yet, the Dhamma teaches us that this craving for control over external conditions is the very source of our suffering (dukkha). When we cling to the idea that we can engineer a permanent, predictable outcome in a world characterized by impermanence (anicca), we create the friction that exhausts our resources and clouds our judgment.

Leaders and innovators often view the "disruption" of their industries as a problem to be solved through better strategy or more advanced tools. However, the teaching of the Four Noble Truths suggests that the problem is not the disruption itself, but our internal reaction to it—our attachment to how things "should be." When we recognize that the market, the state, and even our own technological systems are in a constant state of flux, we can move from a posture of defensive control to one of responsive awareness.

This does not mean passivity. On the contrary, true mastery requires the clarity of mind to act decisively without being enslaved by the outcome. By cultivating mindfulness (sati) in the midst of volatility, we can discern what is truly within our influence and what must be surrendered to the flow of conditions. As the Buddha taught, it is by letting go of the conceit "I am," "this is mine," and "this is my self" regarding these transient phenomena, that one finds lasting peace.

In your professional life, consider your strategic plans. Are they built on the foundation of a stable world, or are they designed to thrive in the reality of an ever-changing one? True strength lies in the ability to remain centered when the world around you is in motion.

Reference: SN 35.1 — The All (Sabba Sutta)
