Dhamma Corner

In the quiet of the morning, we observe the world in flux. Today’s headlines speak of summits in Ankara, shifting energy markets, and the race to define the AI era. To the strategist, these are matters of power, capital, and survival. To the practitioner, they are simply the unfolding of anicca—impermanence. The structures we build, the alliances we forge, and the technologies we deploy are all subject to the same law: they arise, they persist for a time, and they inevitably fade.

Leaders often mistake this persistence for permanence. They believe that if they can just secure the right supply chain, sign the correct memorandum of understanding, or achieve the next level of computational power, they will reach a state of lasting security. Yet, as the Buddha taught, the search for security in external conditions is like trying to grasp the wind. The more tightly we grip, the more we suffer when the conditions inevitably change.

This does not mean we should abandon our work. On the contrary, it invites a higher form of engagement. When you act with the understanding that every outcome is transient, you are freed from the paralysis of fear and the intoxication of ambition. You can build with excellence and precision, yet remain unattached to the result. This is the path of the "wise worker"—one who acts skillfully, responds with clarity, and does not build their identity upon the flickering screens of the market.

Consider the nature of the "Agentic AI" discussed today—systems that plan and execute without human intervention. These are but extensions of our own desire for control, a manifestation of the mind's tendency to want to automate the path to happiness. But peace is not a process to be automated; it is a state to be realized. By observing these developments with equanimity, you remain the master of your attention, rather than a servant to the next disruption.

Personal Reflection

We spend our days calibrating our influence, measuring our success against the backdrop of global shifts, and refining our strategies to ensure we remain relevant in a world that never stops moving. It is easy to view your life as a series of projects to be managed, but what happens when you set the project down? When the screen goes dark and the market closes, who are you? Your ambition is a powerful engine, but it is not your destination. Every choice you make today is a reflection of what you value most—is it the validation of the world, or the clarity of your own mind?

If you were stripped of the titles, the assets, and the influence you have worked so hard to build, what would be left of your sense of purpose?
