Dhamma Corner

In our modern era, we are constantly buffeted by the winds of change—market volatility, geopolitical strife, and the rapid, often disorienting, evolution of technology. We seek stability in indices, in policies, and in the promise of artificial intelligence, hoping these will provide a firm footing for our lives and businesses.

The early Buddhist teachings remind us that this search for external stability is fundamentally flawed because it is predicated on the belief that there is a permanent "self" or "system" to be protected. The Buddha taught the doctrine of Anicca, or impermanence, noting that all conditioned things are in a state of constant flux. When we tie our peace of mind to the success of a technology stock or the outcome of a diplomatic negotiation, we are placing our happiness in the hands of conditions that are, by nature, unstable.

This is not a call for passivity, but for a shift in perspective. A leader who understands that change is the only constant is not paralyzed by the "sharp selloff" or the "fading rally." Instead, they cultivate the ability to act with clarity within the flow of events, rather than being swept away by them. This is the practice of mindfulness applied to strategy—seeing the market as it is, not as we desire it to be, and recognizing that our worth is not tied to the fluctuating numbers on a screen.

By letting go of the desperate need for specific outcomes, we gain the freedom to make better, more ethical, and more sustainable decisions. We become like the water described by the masters—adapting, flowing, and enduring, regardless of the storm.
