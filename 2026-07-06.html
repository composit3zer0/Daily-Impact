Dhamma Corner

In the rapid-fire world of geopolitical shifts, AI infrastructure, and market volatility, the mind often seeks refuge in the promise of future stability. We look to the summit in Ankara, the potential government partnerships, or the resilience of regional markets, hoping that if we can just align these external forces correctly, we will find peace. Yet, the Dhamma teaches that this pursuit of external order is a pursuit of a mirage.

The Buddha frequently pointed to the nature of *anicca*—impermanence. Whether it is the rise of a technology giant or the shifting alliances of nations, these are conditioned phenomena. They arise, they persist for a time, and they pass away. When a decision-maker or an innovator anchors their sense of self or security in these shifting conditions, they are inevitably subject to *dukkha*, or unsatisfactoriness. The stress of the market is not inherent in the market itself, but in the mind’s demand that it remain a certain way.

For the leader or the nomad navigating this era, the practice is not to abandon the world, but to cultivate a mind that is "like a pillar," as the Buddha described in the *Cula-Malunkyovada Sutta*. A pillar is not shaken by the wind, not because it fights the wind, but because it is firmly rooted. To be rooted in the Dhamma is to recognize the nature of the "wind"—the news, the trends, the geopolitical tensions—without being defined by them. It is the ability to engage with the world with full intelligence and compassion, while maintaining the inner clarity that understands the ultimate nature of these events.

Do not be deceived by the complexity of the systems you navigate. The complexity is merely the surface of the water; the depth remains undisturbed. By cultivating the stillness of the mind, you gain the capacity to make decisions that are not reactive, but responsive—aligned with the truth of the moment rather than the fear of the future.

Reference: Cula-Malunkyovada Sutta (MN 63) — The Shorter Discourse to Malunkya
Personal Reflection

Today, you are bombarded with headlines about power—who has it, who is losing it, and how it is being restructured through technology and alliances. It is easy to view these events as things happening "out there," separate from you. But consider the power you exercise every hour: the power of your attention.

When you choose to focus on a specific risk or a potential opportunity, you are literally constructing your reality. You are deciding what is significant and what is noise. Are you choosing your focus, or is your focus being chosen for you by the urgency of the news?

If you were to reclaim your attention from the stories that demand it, what would you choose to look at instead?
