Dhamma Corner

In today's world, we see a frantic search for security. Whether it is the market's reaction to interest rates, the geopolitical maneuvering in the Caucasus, or the fear of a leak on the space station, the underlying human impulse is the same: to create a permanent, stable condition where no harm can reach us. We build our portfolios, our alliances, and our defenses, believing that if we just get the variables right, we will be safe.

The teaching of the Dhamma points to a different reality: anicca, or impermanence. Nothing in the conditioned world is static. The market that rose for ten weeks is now falling; the geopolitics that seemed settled are now shifting; even the metal we thought we understood—cobalt—reveals hidden complexities we never saw before. To cling to the idea that we can engineer a permanent state of safety is to struggle against the fundamental nature of existence.

For those navigating the complexities of leadership and innovation, the invitation is not to abandon planning, but to cultivate a mind that is not dependent on the outcome. When you work, work with precision and care, as if the project were permanent, but hold the result with the lightness of knowing it is already changing. This is the practice of upekkhā, or equanimity. It is the ability to engage fully with the world—to lead, to innovate, to invest—without the desperate, anxious grasping that turns a professional challenge into a personal crisis.

By understanding that the 'ground' we stand on is always moving, we can learn to move with it, rather than being broken by it. This is the path of true resilience.
