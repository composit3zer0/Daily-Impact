Dhamma Corner

In today's world, we see the frantic oscillations of markets, the signing of treaties, and the race for dominance in artificial intelligence. We witness the rise and fall of fortunes, the shifting of power, and the constant, restless pursuit of security. Yet, the early Buddhist teachings offer a steadying perspective on this turbulence: the understanding of *anicca*, or impermanence.

Leaders and innovators often operate under the assumption that they can secure a permanent state of stability through policy, technology, or strategic alliances. However, the Buddha taught that all conditioned things are subject to change. The peace deal in the Middle East, while welcome, is a conditioned phenomenon—it is dependent on causes and conditions that are themselves in flux. To cling to the hope that this state will remain static is to invite future suffering when conditions inevitably shift again.

For the decision-maker or the digital nomad, the practice is not to abandon strategy, but to engage with it without the heavy burden of attachment. When you build a business, an AI platform, or a trade network, do so with the clarity that these are temporary configurations. When you succeed, do not be intoxicated by the gain; when you face a market correction or a geopolitical shock, do not be devastated by the loss. This is the path of the wise—to act with full vigor and intelligence, while remaining internally unattached to the outcome.

The Buddha emphasized the importance of *yoniso manasikara*, or appropriate attention. In an age of information overload and rapid change, your most valuable asset is the ability to see things as they truly are, rather than through the lens of fear, ambition, or reactivity. By cultivating this stillness, you become more effective, more resilient, and more capable of navigating the inevitable waves of change that define our global landscape.

Reference: SN 35.1 — The All (Sabba Sutta)
Personal Reflection

You spend your days building, optimizing, and securing your place in a world that is shifting under your feet. You seek the "peace" of a stable portfolio, a secure career, or a predictable future. But look closely at the moments when you feel most anxious. Is it because the world is changing, or because you are trying to hold onto a version of yourself that no longer exists? True power is not found in the stability of your circumstances, but in the spaciousness of your awareness as you watch them change.
