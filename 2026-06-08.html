Dhamma Corner

In a world characterized by rapid technological expansion and geopolitical friction, the mind often seeks refuge in the promise of future stability—the next breakthrough, the next market cycle, or the next strategic alliance. Yet, as the Buddha taught, this pursuit of external security is a reliance on that which is inherently unstable. The phenomena we observe today—the shifting of global powers, the rapid deployment of autonomous systems, and the volatility of markets—are expressions of anicca, or impermanence.

Leaders and innovators often operate under the illusion that by mastering the mechanisms of the world—the code, the supply chains, the policy frameworks—they can secure a lasting outcome. However, the teaching of anatta, or non-self, reminds us that no system, organization, or personal ambition can be held as a permanent, unchanging core. When we attach our identity to our achievements or our strategic predictions, we invite suffering the moment these conditions inevitably shift.

The practice for the modern leader is not to withdraw from the world, but to engage with a mind that is unattached. By maintaining awareness of the impermanent nature of these digital and political structures, one can act with clarity and compassion rather than reactive desperation. When the news speaks of conflict or market rotation, the wise observe these events as one watches a river—recognizing the movement without trying to grasp the water. This is the path to inner clarity: to be deeply engaged in the work of the world while remaining fundamentally undisturbed by its changing tides.
