Dhamma Corner

The world today is characterized by rapid, often violent fluctuations—the sudden surge of oil prices, the collapse of market expectations, and the hardening of geopolitical positions. These events, while appearing external and beyond our control, are rooted in the fundamental nature of conditioned existence (sankhara). As long as we cling to the idea that stability is a permanent state, we will always be distressed by the inevitable shift of the tides.

In the early teachings, the Buddha consistently emphasized the nature of impermanence (anicca). When we view market trends or international relations through the lens of permanent expectations, we suffer because our desires are at odds with reality. The wise leader or investor does not seek to prevent change, for change is the nature of the world. Instead, they cultivate a mind that is steady amidst the flux.

This is not a call to passivity. On the contrary, it is a call to profound clarity. When the mind is not clouded by the fever of craving or the smoke of aversion, one can act with precision. In the *Sallatha Sutta*, the Buddha teaches that the uninstructed person feels the "darts" of worldly change and reacts with further craving, essentially shooting a second dart into themselves. The instructed person, however, understands the nature of the first dart—the unavoidable event—but refuses to shoot the second—the internal reaction of fear or desperate grasping.

For those navigating the complexities of modern innovation and global strategy, the practice is to observe the "darts" of market volatility and geopolitical friction without internalizing them as personal threats to your identity or success. By maintaining this distance, you remain agile, capable of making decisions that are not reactive, but responsive to the actual conditions as they arise.
