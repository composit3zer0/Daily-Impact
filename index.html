Dhamma Corner

In the current global climate of shifting alliances and economic instability, the mind naturally seeks refuge in the familiar. When the Strait of Hormuz faces disruption or energy prices oscillate, we feel a constriction—a tightening of the heart that says, "This must be stable for me to be at peace." Yet, the Dhamma teaches that this reliance on external conditions is the very root of our restlessness.

In the *Sabbasava Sutta*, the Buddha speaks of the necessity of restraining the senses and reflecting on the nature of our attachments. He notes that when we are swayed by the "cankers" of craving for existence and the desire for material security, we become vulnerable to every change in the environment. For the leader or the innovator, this does not imply a retreat from action. Rather, it suggests a profound shift in orientation: acting with full clarity while remaining unattached to the outcome.

When you encounter a supply chain failure or a geopolitical crisis, observe the immediate impulse to panic. This is not a failure of strategy; it is a manifestation of the mind's tendency to grasp at permanence. By recognizing this, you can move from a state of reactive anxiety to one of responsive intelligence. You act because it is necessary, not because you are enslaved to the preservation of a specific status quo.

As you navigate the volatility of 2026, remember that the "resilience" so often discussed in business is a reflection of the inner quality of *upekkha*, or equanimity. It is the ability to remain balanced amidst the eight worldly winds: gain and loss, status and disgrace, censure and praise, pleasure and pain. True strategic advantage belongs to those who, like the lotus, remain rooted but unspotted by the muddy waters of the world.

Ref: MN 2: Sabbāsava Sutta — All the Cankers
