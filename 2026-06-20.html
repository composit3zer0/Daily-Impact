Dhamma Corner

In a world defined by the rapid movement of capital, the hardening of geopolitical borders, and the rise of autonomous systems, the mind often seeks refuge in the stability of external structures. We look to treaties, to market regulations, and to technological safeguards to provide the security we crave. Yet, the Buddha’s teaching consistently points to the impermanence of all conditioned things (sabbe saṅkhārā aniccā).

The current tensions surrounding the Strait of Hormuz or the shifting alliances within Southeast Asia serve as modern manifestations of saṅkhāra—compounded processes that are constantly in flux. When we, as leaders or innovators, view these developments as solid, controllable entities, we suffer when they inevitably shift. We become anxious, reactive, or trapped in the "shadow" of our own projections.

True intelligence in this era does not mean predicting the future with absolute certainty, for such certainty is an illusion. Instead, it is the cultivation of a steady, discerning awareness that can operate within the flow of change. By practicing sati (mindfulness) and paññā (wisdom), we learn to observe these global shifts not as personal threats, but as phenomena to be understood. When the market surges or a policy is reversed, the grounded mind remains unswayed by the turbulence, allowing for decisions made with clarity rather than panic.

The practice is to find the "still point" within the movement. As you navigate the complex systems of 2026, remember that the most resilient technology is not an algorithm, but a mind that understands the nature of change itself. By letting go of the desperate need for specific outcomes, you gain the freedom to act skillfully in the present moment, which is the only place where true influence resides.
