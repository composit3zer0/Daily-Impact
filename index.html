Dhamma Corner

In a world defined by the rapid oscillation between geopolitical crisis and technological fervor, the mind easily becomes a mirror reflecting the turbulence of the external environment. We see the reopening of a shipping lane and feel relief; we see a military review and feel anxiety. These fluctuations are the nature of the conditioned world, a realm the Buddha described as inherently unstable.

The teaching of "Dependent Origination" reminds us that all things arise in reliance upon conditions. The Strait of Hormuz is not inherently "open" or "closed"; it is a phenomenon dependent on the shifting intentions of leaders, the state of global energy consumption, and the movement of vessels. When we grasp at these conditions, hoping for a permanent state of security or progress, we invite suffering. The innovator, the investor, and the leader are all subject to this law.

True resilience for the high-value decision-maker is not found in controlling the external variables—which is ultimately impossible—but in developing the "unshakeable mind." In the *Cula-Malunkyovada Sutta*, the Buddha illustrates the futility of obsessing over questions that do not lead to the cessation of suffering. When we are struck by the arrow of market volatility or geopolitical threat, our task is not to speculate on the infinite complexities of the "why" or the "what if," but to attend to the immediate, skillful action required in the present moment.

This is the practice of "Right Effort." It is the ability to engage with the technology of the future and the diplomacy of the present without losing one's center. When you are navigating the complexities of your industry, ask yourself: Is my attention serving the task at hand, or is it being consumed by the flickering shadows of the news cycle? By anchoring the mind in the present, you gain the clarity to make decisions that are not reactive, but responsive—rooted in wisdom rather than the fever of the moment.
