Dhamma Corner

In the rapid currents of today's global economy—where AI-driven growth clashes with the heavy friction of geopolitical conflict—the mind often seeks a fixed point of safety. We look to secure supply chains, hedge against energy volatility, and build robust structures to insulate our work from the unpredictability of the world. Yet, the early teachings of the Buddha remind us that the attempt to find absolute stability in the external world is a fundamental misunderstanding of the nature of existence.

The world is described as anicca, or impermanent. When we observe the rise and fall of semiconductor stocks or the shifting tides of maritime trade, we are witnessing the inherent instability of conditioned things. The suffering—or dukkha—that we experience as leaders and innovators often stems not from the volatility itself, but from the desperate desire for these phenomena to be otherwise. We wish for the market to be predictable, for the supply chain to be seamless, and for our influence to be lasting. When these conditions inevitably shift, we feel the impact as a personal or professional failure.

The path to inner clarity lies in developing an equanimous awareness of these changes. Just as a captain does not fight the ocean but adjusts to its swells, the leader must cultivate an ability to act effectively without being swept away by the emotional weight of uncertainty. This is not passive resignation; it is the highest form of strategic intelligence. By recognizing that all systems—whether economic, political, or personal—are in a state of constant flux, one can make decisions with greater precision and less attachment to fixed outcomes.

When you encounter the next disruption, pause. Observe the impulse to grasp for control or the fear of loss. These are not external events; they are movements within your own consciousness. By seeing them as they are, you create the space required for true innovation. The goal is to remain centered in the midst of the storm, allowing the wisdom of the present moment to guide your strategy, rather than the anxiety of what might be lost.
