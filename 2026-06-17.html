Dhamma Corner

In a world characterized by the rapid oscillation between conflict and fragile peace—as seen in the current developments in the Strait of Hormuz—the mind often seeks stability in external conditions. We look to geopolitical agreements, market corrections, and technological infrastructure as sources of security. Yet, the Buddha teaches that these external phenomena are inherently unstable (*anicca*). To anchor one's peace to the reopening of a shipping lane or the rise of an AI index is to build a house on shifting sands.

The teaching on the Eight Worldly Conditions (*lokadhamma*)—gain and loss, status and disgrace, blame and praise, pleasure and pain—is particularly relevant for those navigating high-stakes environments. A leader or innovator who is swayed by these fluctuations becomes a servant to the very systems they seek to master. By recognizing that these conditions are not within our ultimate control, one develops the equanimity required to act decisively without being consumed by the outcome.

True resilience for the modern decision-maker is not found in the mastery of the market, but in the mastery of the attention. When the mind is trained to observe the arising and passing of thoughts and reactions to external events, one gains the clarity to see the "spiral" Leonardo da Vinci noted—the repetitive patterns of human ambition and fear. By stepping back from the immediate identification with these patterns, one cultivates a sense of *upekkhā* (equanimity) that is not indifference, but a profound engagement with reality as it is.

As you navigate the complexities of your work, consider the practice of *sati* (mindfulness) as a way to maintain your center amidst the volatility. It is not about retreating from the world, but about engaging with it from a foundation of internal stability. When the external world is in flux, the internal world can remain a source of calm intelligence.
