Dhamma Corner

In the rapid flux of global markets, where nations vie for control over critical minerals and AI dominance, the mind often seeks refuge in the illusion of permanence. We see a world of shifting alliances and economic volatility, yet we react as if we could anchor our identity and security in these external, changing conditions. The Buddha taught that this pursuit is the root of suffering. When we identify with the success of a venture, the stability of a supply chain, or the influence of a nation, we are clinging to the "constructed"—the *sankhara*—which is inherently unstable.

Leaders and innovators today are tasked with navigating immense complexity. The Dhamma does not suggest withdrawing from these challenges, but rather approaching them with a mind that understands the nature of *anicca* (impermanence). When a project fails or a market shifts, the suffering that arises is not caused by the event itself, but by our rigid expectation that it should have remained fixed. By recognizing that these systems are in constant flux, a leader can act with greater agility, making decisions based on clarity rather than fear or attachment.

The practice is to develop a "center" that is not contingent on external outcomes. This is not apathy; it is the highest form of strategic engagement. When you are not personally diminished by the failure of a strategy or the rise of a competitor, you possess the freedom to act with compassion and wisdom. You see the causal links—the *paticcasamuppada* (dependent origination)—that others miss because their vision is clouded by the desire for a specific, permanent result.

As you navigate your day, observe the impulse to control the uncontrollable. Notice when you are trying to force a result to justify your own sense of self. Return to the breath, return to the present moment, and recognize that your power lies not in your ability to stop the change, but in your ability to remain steady within it. True innovation arises from a mind that is spacious enough to hold the reality of constant change without breaking.

Reference: SN 35.1: The Teaching on Impermanence (Samyutta Nikaya)
