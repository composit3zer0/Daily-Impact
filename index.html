Dhamma Corner

In a world accelerating toward automated decision-making, the early Buddhist teachings on yoniso manasikāra—wise or appropriate attention—become a vital survival skill. We are surrounded by agents like Microsoft's Scout or Apple's new Siri, designed to anticipate our needs and streamline our choices. While these tools promise liberation from minutiae, they also threaten to outsource the very faculty of discernment that the Buddha emphasized as the root of wisdom.

In the Vitakkasaṇṭhāna Sutta, the Buddha provides a framework for managing the unruly mind. He teaches that when unwholesome thoughts or distractions arise, one should not merely react, but observe the process and redirect attention. For the modern leader, the "unwholesome" is not just a passing thought, but the passive absorption of algorithmic suggestions. If we allow the machine to decide our priorities, we lose the capacity to see the dhamma—the way things truly are—independent of commercial framing.

True intelligence is not found in the speed of an agentic workflow, but in the clarity of the intention behind it. When the Kālāmas asked the Buddha how to discern truth amidst conflicting claims, he did not offer a tool or an algorithm. He directed them back to their own experience: examine what leads to harm, what is skillful, and what is unskillful. This is the ultimate "human-in-the-loop" system. Your identity is not defined by the efficiency of your AI, but by the quality of the attention you bring to the present moment.

Source: Vitakkasaṇṭhāna Sutta: The Dispersing of Thoughts (MN 20)
Personal Reflection

We often treat our attention as a limitless resource, yet the news cycle and our new digital assistants are designed to capture it with surgical precision. When you delegate your routine tasks to an AI, you are not just saving time; you are potentially outsourcing the very friction that forces you to pause, reflect, and choose. If you never have to struggle with a decision, how do you know what you truly value? The ease of the future is a gift, but it is also a quiet invitation to sleepwalk through your own life.

If your tools could handle every decision you make today, which one would you refuse to give up?
