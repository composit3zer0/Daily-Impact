Dhamma Corner

In the modern landscape of rapid geopolitical shifts and the relentless drive for technological dominance, we often find ourselves caught in the grip of craving—the desire for more market share, more security, more certainty. The Buddha, in his teachings, consistently pointed to the nature of "becoming" (bhava) as a source of suffering. When we identify our personal or corporate identity entirely with our expansion, our innovations, or our influence, we tether ourselves to impermanence.

The recent tensions in the Strait of Hormuz and the race for AI supremacy are reflections of a world operating under the assumption that "more" will eventually lead to "enough." Yet, the Dhamma teaches that the world is in constant flux (anicca). The struggle to control external conditions is, by definition, a struggle against reality itself. For the high-value decision-maker, the practice is not to abandon ambition, but to decouple one's inner stability from the volatility of external outcomes.

This does not imply passivity. Rather, it suggests a profound strategic clarity. When you are no longer driven by the fear of losing your position or the frantic need to secure the next advantage, you can see the landscape as it truly is, rather than how your biases and anxieties paint it. By recognizing that even the most powerful nations and the most advanced technologies are subject to the same laws of change, one can act with a sense of detachment that is, paradoxically, more effective than the reactive posturing of those who are enslaved by their goals.

The path toward sustainable influence—whether in governance, technology, or finance—is paved with the understanding of dependent origination. Nothing arises in isolation. Your success is dependent on a vast web of conditions; recognizing this fosters a collaborative, rather than a purely adversarial, approach to global challenges. When you act with this awareness, you move from the role of a desperate architect of your own empire to a compassionate steward of a shared reality.

As you navigate the pressures of this day, remember that the most resilient structure is not the one that resists the storm, but the one that understands its own nature as part of the storm.
