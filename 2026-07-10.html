Dhamma Corner

In the rapid flux of 2026—where global growth is recalibrated by conflict and the promise of physical AI—the Buddha’s teaching on the nature of "becoming" (bhava) remains a vital anchor for the decision-maker. We often perceive our projects, our supply chains, and our technological advancements as solid, permanent entities. We invest in them, we defend them, and we fear their disruption.

The Dhamma reminds us that this perception of solidity is a construct. Everything we build—whether a nation’s energy policy, a robotics framework, or a corporate strategy—is subject to the same laws of dependent origination. Nothing arises in isolation. The IMF’s growth forecast isn't just a number; it is a reflection of thousands of interconnected conditions, from the flow of oil in Hormuz to the investment cycles in Silicon Valley. When we cling to the idea that these conditions should remain static, we invite suffering.

For the leader or innovator, the practice is not to abandon ambition, but to act with "Right View." This means seeing the volatility of the market not as a personal crisis, but as the natural manifestation of an interconnected, changing system. When you understand that the system is inherently unstable, you stop fighting the reality of the change and start working skillfully within it. You become like water, as the editorial roundtable suggested—not by being passive, but by adapting your strategy to the shifting ground.

The Buddha taught the importance of staying grounded in the present moment, even while navigating the complexities of the world. This is not an invitation to ignore the future or neglect your responsibilities. Rather, it is an invitation to perform your duties without the burden of excessive attachment to the outcome. When you can detach your sense of identity from the success or failure of your market strategy, you gain a unique clarity. You see the risks more clearly, you act more decisively, and you remain resilient in the face of the inevitable turbulence of our era.

As you move through your day, observe the impulse to control the uncontrollable. Recognize the urge to secure your position against the tide. By letting go of the need for the world to conform to your plans, you find the space to respond with wisdom rather than reaction.
