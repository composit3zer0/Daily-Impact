Dhamma Corner

In a world characterized by rapid technological integration—where KPMG deploys frontier AI to hundreds of thousands of professionals and industries race to redefine their core operations—we find ourselves in a state of constant, accelerating change. The Buddha often taught that the nature of all compounded things is to arise and pass away. When we tether our identity or our sense of security to these external systems, we inevitably experience suffering (dukkha) because those systems, like all phenomena, are subject to impermanence.

Leaders and innovators today are tasked with managing immense complexity. The temptation is to believe that by creating more sophisticated tools, we can finally achieve a state of perfect control or "readiness." Yet, the Teaching reminds us that control is an illusion. The "tactical imperative" of the modern world often masks a deeper craving for stability in a volatile environment. True readiness, however, is not found in the robustness of our software or the scale of our infrastructure, but in the cultivation of a mind that is not shaken by the fluctuations of the external world.

When you encounter the "K-shaped" realities of today's economy or the geopolitical tensions mentioned in our reports, view them through the lens of the Four Noble Truths. The stress and tension you feel are not merely reflections of external events; they are the result of clinging to the idea that things should be otherwise. By recognizing the transient nature of these crises, you can engage with the world with greater clarity and compassion, acting with purpose rather than reacting from fear.

Innovation, when guided by the principles of right action and right intention, becomes a service to the collective rather than a tool for dominance. As you navigate your own professional landscape, remember that the most sophisticated AI is no substitute for the wisdom of a calm, centered, and ethical mind. Practice the art of letting go, even while you build. This is the path to true resilience.

Source: SN 35.136: The Simile of the Snake (Samyutta Nikaya) — A reflection on the dangers of grasping at that which is impermanent.
