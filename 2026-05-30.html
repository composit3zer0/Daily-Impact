Dhamma Corner

In an era defined by record-breaking market highs, aggressive technological expansion, and the constant friction of geopolitical realignment, the mind is frequently pulled into a state of perpetual anticipation. We wait for the next earnings report, the next signing of a treaty, or the next breakthrough in AI, believing that stability—or the ultimate success—is just around the corner.

The teachings of the Buddha remind us that this very structure of expectation is the root of our restlessness. In the Sabbasava Sutta, the discourse on the taints, the Buddha teaches the importance of wise attention—not just to the external developments of the world, but to the internal mechanics of our own craving and aversion.

For the leader or the innovator, the "taints" are often disguised as ambition. When we identify entirely with the success of a venture or the outcome of a negotiation, we lose the fluidity that allows us to respond skillfully to adversity. The world will always be in flux; wars will be negotiated, markets will cycle, and technologies will evolve. The Dhamma does not ask us to withdraw from these spheres, but to engage with them from a position of non-attachment.

When the market surges, the wise practitioner recognizes it as a passing condition, neither inherently "good" nor "ours." When obstacles arise, as with the technical failures in space exploration or the complexities of international trade, the mind remains unruffled by the desire for a different reality. By cultivating this stillness, we make better decisions—not out of fear or greed, but out of clear, compassionate understanding of what is actually required in the present moment.

Personal Reflection

You are currently operating in a world that demands you be everywhere at once—tracking the markets, navigating the geopolitical currents, and mastering the latest AI tools. It is easy to feel as though you are the sum of these external pressures. Yet, beneath the noise of the "record-breaking" headlines and the "critical" deadlines, there is a quiet space that does not change. That space is not built on performance or output; it simply is. When you strip away the identity of the "market mover" or the "innovator," what remains is the person who must choose, every day, how to be present in the midst of the chaos.
