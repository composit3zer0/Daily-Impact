Dhamma Corner

In the rapid flux of 2026—where AI agents accelerate market cycles, currencies fluctuate, and geopolitical tensions rise—the human mind often seeks a static point of reference. We look for stability in the performance of assets, the durability of supply chains, or the predictability of geopolitical alliances. Yet, the Buddha’s teaching on *Anicca*, or impermanence, suggests that the very ground of our professional and personal lives is in constant motion.

The Dhammapada reminds us that those who hold onto things, believing they will provide lasting security, are like people grasping at clouds. This is not a call to apathy or to abandon one's responsibilities as a leader or innovator. On the contrary, it is an invitation to engage with the world with greater clarity. When you accept that the market, the technology, and the political landscape are inherently transient, you are no longer paralyzed by the fear of their shifting nature.

For the decision-maker, this means shifting focus from controlling outcomes—which are beyond our ultimate grasp—to refining the quality of attention and intention behind each choice. In the *Mahaparinibbana Sutta*, the Buddha’s final instruction encourages his followers to be "islands unto themselves," finding refuge in the Dhamma rather than in external circumstances. For the modern professional, this "island" is a state of inner stillness and ethical integrity, which remains unshaken whether the rupiah falls or a new technology disrupts an entire industry.

Innovation, when detached from the thirst for personal gain or the fear of status loss, becomes a form of service. When you build, you build because it is the right action in the present moment, not because you are clutching at a future that has not yet arrived. This is the essence of *Appamada*, or heedfulness—a wakeful, diligent engagement with the world as it is, not as we wish it to be. In the face of global instability, this is the most strategic posture one can adopt.

Reference: Mahaparinibbana Sutta (DN 16) — The Great Passing
