Dhamma Corner

In a world defined by the volatility of markets, the tension of geopolitical conflict, and the relentless pursuit of technological growth, we often find ourselves caught in the grip of craving—a hunger for stability, for profit, or for security. The early Buddhist teachings remind us that this very hunger is the root of our agitation. When we look at the headlines of today, we see the unfolding of anicca, or impermanence. The trade routes we rely upon, the technologies we deem essential, and the political alliances we trust are all subject to change. They are conditioned phenomena, rising and falling according to causes and conditions.

For the leader or innovator, the practice is not to abandon the world or its challenges, but to engage with them from a position of inner stability. When the market shifts, or when supply chains are disrupted, the untrained mind reacts with fear or grasping. The trained mind, however, observes the change without becoming identified with it. As the Buddha taught, when we cling to these external conditions as if they were permanent sources of happiness or identity, we invite suffering when they inevitably shift.

This is not a call for passivity. Rather, it is a call for a strategic detachment. By recognizing the transient nature of all worldly success, you gain the clarity to make decisions that are not driven by panic or greed, but by the reality of the situation at hand. True power lies not in controlling the world—which is impossible—but in mastering the mind that perceives it. When you are no longer a slave to the outcome, you become a master of the process, capable of navigating the chaos with a calm, compassionate, and steady hand.

Source: SN 35.1 — The All (Sabba Sutta)
Personal Reflection

You spend much of your day optimizing for efficiency—speeding up workflows, securing assets, and predicting the next move in a volatile market. But in your rush to secure the future, how often do you pause to acknowledge the present? We often treat our current circumstances as mere obstacles to be overcome on the way to a "better" version of ourselves or our businesses. Yet, the friction you feel today is not just a problem to solve; it is the fabric of your existence right now. What if your value is not in the outcome, but in how you hold the uncertainty?

If you could strip away the need to control the next five years, what would you choose to do with your attention today?
