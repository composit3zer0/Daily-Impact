Dhamma Corner

In the rapid-fire world of enterprise AI, global military strikes, and vanishing glacial landscapes, the mind is constantly pulled toward the future—calculating risks, predicting outcomes, and grasping for stability. Yet, the Buddha consistently pointed to the futility of chasing a future that is inherently unstable.

The teaching on the "All" (Sabba Sutta) provides a radical framework for the modern decision-maker. When we identify with our digital tools, our market positions, or our geopolitical narratives, we are merely identifying with phenomena that are, by nature, subject to change. The stress arises not from the events themselves—the drone strikes or the market volatility—but from the attempt to hold onto these phenomena as if they were solid, permanent, or "ours."

For the innovator or the leader, true clarity comes from recognizing that the "All" is limited to the six sense bases and their objects. When you look at a dashboard of performance metrics or a geopolitical briefing, you are interacting with *dhamma* (phenomena). If you can observe these inputs without the reflexive grasping—the need for them to be different, the fear of them disappearing—you gain the space to act with precision rather than reaction. This is the difference between being a slave to the news cycle and being an architect of meaningful change.

Consider the "five reinvigorations" mentioned in recent diplomatic efforts. While these proposals seek to structure the world, the Dhamma teaches that no system, no matter how well-designed, can escape the law of impermanence. The wise leader works within these systems but does not place their ultimate reliance upon them. By letting go of the need for the world to conform to our desires, we find a steady ground that no market crash or military conflict can shake.

Personal Reflection

Today’s news is a relentless stream of "what if": What if the peace deal fails? What if the AI model hallucinates? What if the glaciers disappear? You spend much of your day navigating these potential futures, trying to secure a version of yourself that is safe, successful, and relevant. But this constant projection steals your presence. The anxiety you feel about the market or the world is often just a proxy for a deeper fear of losing control over your own trajectory.

If you were not busy preparing for a future that may never arrive, who would you be right now?
