Dhamma Corner

In a world defined by Metaruptions—where geopolitical friction, technological acceleration, and economic uncertainty collide—the mind often seeks refuge in the illusion of control. We look at the Strait of Hormuz or the volatility of AI stocks and feel that if we could just calculate the outcome, we would be secure. Yet, the Buddha taught that the nature of all conditioned things is impermanent (anicca), unsatisfactory (dukkha), and devoid of a fixed, separate self (anatta).

The anxiety felt by investors today is a form of clinging to a future that has not yet arrived. When we invest, we are essentially placing our intention into a future state. The Dhamma encourages us to perform our duties with diligence and skill, yet remain unattached to the results. This is not passive resignation; it is the highest form of strategic clarity. When the mind is free from the fever of 'I' and 'mine'—the desire for a specific stock to rise, or for a specific geopolitical outcome—the ability to act with precision is enhanced.

The *Sabbasava Sutta* reminds us that the uninstructed worldling is troubled by thoughts of "Did I exist in the past?" or "What will I become in the future?" Leaders and innovators often fall into this trap, obsessing over the "what ifs" of the next quarter or the next election. Instead, practice the cessation of these mental proliferations. By seeing the world as it actually is—a flux of interdependent events—you become a more effective agent of change. You move through the volatility not as a victim of the storm, but as one who understands that the storm is simply the nature of the elements.

Personal Reflection

Today’s news is a cascade of external forces—blockades, AI pivots, and diplomatic missions. It is easy to feel that your identity is tied to the success or failure of these macro events. You are the one who decides where to place your attention, yet you often let the noise choose for you. Ask yourself: if the external world were suddenly to go silent, what ambition would remain? Is your current drive a reflection of your true purpose, or are you simply running because the world is running?
