Dhamma Corner

In today's global landscape, we observe a frantic race for certainty. Governments, corporations, and investors are all attempting to build 'fortresses'—whether through AI-driven surveillance, strategic energy alliances, or institutionalizing new financial assets. There is a deep, underlying fear that if we do not control the future, it will consume us. This is the classic movement of the uninstructed mind: seeking to secure the impermanent.

The Buddha taught that the nature of all conditioned things is to change, to arise and pass away. When we attempt to force stability upon a system that is inherently fluid—such as global markets, geopolitical alliances, or technological trends—we only create more friction, more anxiety, and more suffering. The effort to 'crush' an opponent or 'secure' a supply chain is often a reaction to the fear of loss.

For the decision-maker or the innovator, the Dhamma does not suggest inaction. Rather, it suggests a shift in the quality of the action. When one acts from a place of attachment—attachment to a specific outcome, to an identity, or to a market position—the mind becomes contracted. It loses the ability to see the system as it truly is, seeing instead only what it hopes for or fears. True intelligence, or wisdom, arises when we can engage with the world’s complexity without being tethered to the outcome.

Consider the 'agentic' systems being built today. They are designed to act on our behalf. But if the underlying intent is driven by fear, the agency will only scale that fear. If the intent is rooted in clarity, the agency can be a tool for genuine benefit. To lead in such times requires the capacity to observe the 'rising and falling' of markets and geopolitical tides with the same equanimity one brings to the breath in meditation. We must build, yes, but we must build without the delusion that we are building anything that will last forever.

Personal Reflection

You have likely spent the last few hours processing information that is designed to create a sense of urgency. The markets are shifting, conflicts are escalating, and new technologies are promising to reshape your life. There is a quiet, persistent pressure to respond, to decide, and to secure your position in this moving landscape.

But consider this: the urgency you feel is a narrative you are choosing to adopt. You are reacting to the speed of the world, but your internal state does not have to be a reflection of that speed. When you pause, you are not falling behind; you are simply recalibrating your perspective. Most of what seems vital today will be forgotten by next month. What remains is not the market data or the geopolitical headlines, but the quality of your attention and the integrity of your choices.

Today, try to notice the moments when you feel the need to "secure" something—a decision, an opinion, a result. Ask yourself what you are truly afraid of losing in that moment. There is a profound freedom in recognizing that you can participate in the world without being owned by its volatility.

Where in your work today are you acting out of fear of loss, rather than from a clear vision of what is actually needed?
