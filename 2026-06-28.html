Dhamma Corner

In a world gripped by sudden shifts—from the volatility of tech stocks to the physical ruptures of the earth—the human mind instinctively seeks a solid foundation. We look for stability in assets, in borders, and in the continuity of our systems. Yet, the early teachings of the Buddha invite us to look closer at the nature of this "solid" world.

The doctrine of *anicca*, or impermanence, is not a pessimistic observation but a strategic reality. When we cling to the idea that the market, our career, or our physical safety should remain constant, we create the very friction that causes suffering. The vendor in Bangkok, facing rising costs, and the investor in New York, watching their portfolio rotate, are both experiencing the same fundamental truth: the conditions of the world are in constant flux.

The path for the leader or the innovator is not to attempt to stop the flow of change, but to develop the mental agility to move with it. This is the practice of *upekkha*, or equanimity—not an indifferent detachment, but a profound balance in the face of the changing winds. By recognizing that the "success" or "failure" of today's news is merely a transient arrangement of conditions, one can make decisions with clarity rather than panic.

As the Buddha taught, those who perceive the arising and passing away of phenomena do not become entangled in them. This is the ultimate competitive advantage for the decision-maker: the ability to act decisively when action is required, yet remain unburdened by the outcome. When you let go of the need for the world to be a certain way, you become free to see it exactly as it is, allowing you to navigate the most turbulent markets with a steady hand.
