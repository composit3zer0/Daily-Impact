Dhamma Corner

In a world fixated on the trillion-dollar milestone and the rapid acceleration of agentic AI, we often find ourselves caught in the grip of tanha—the craving for more, for faster, for greater control. We treat the market as an external force that dictates our reality, forgetting that the landscape we navigate is, at its heart, a projection of collective human intent.

The Buddha taught that the root of our suffering—our anxiety over market volatility, our fear of geopolitical instability, our ambition for technological dominance—lies in the delusion of a permanent, independent "self" that must be defended and expanded. When we build infrastructure, whether digital or physical, we are essentially building monuments to this delusion. We seek to stabilize the world to provide comfort to a "self" that is, by its very nature, in constant flux.

The Dhamma invites us to see the "Agentic Economy" not as a new frontier of power, but as a mirror of our own unsettled minds. If the AI we create is chaotic, it is because we have not yet mastered the chaos within. The strategic innovator, the leader, the investor—each must realize that true stability cannot be found in a balance sheet or a market cap. It is found in the stillness of a mind that has let go of the need to grasp.

As the Cula-Malunkyovada Sutta reminds us, we must be careful not to get distracted by speculative questions—"Is the world eternal?" or "Is the AI sentient?"—while we are struck by the arrow of suffering. The urgent task is not to solve the mystery of the machine, but to attend to the quality of our own attention. When you are making a decision, are you acting from a place of clear, compassionate awareness, or are you reacting to the fear of missing out? The digital nomad and the CEO alike find peace not when they have conquered the market, but when they have ceased to be a slave to the craving for it.
