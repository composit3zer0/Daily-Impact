Dhamma Corner

In today's headlines, we observe a world defined by volatility: geopolitical threats, the sudden disruption of energy corridors, and the frantic race for technological supremacy. For the decision-maker or innovator, these events are not merely external data points; they are manifestations of the fundamental nature of conditioned existence. The Dhamma teaches that all things that arise are subject to the law of impermanence (anicca). Whether it is a market trend, an export directive, or a geopolitical ceasefire, these are transient phenomena, shifting according to conditions.

Leaders often fall into the trap of believing they can secure permanent stability through control—be it through tolls, sanctions, or the accumulation of proprietary AI models. Yet, the Buddha observed that the attempt to grasp that which is inherently unstable is the root of suffering. When we identify our identity or our success with these shifting conditions, we become fragile. When the conditions change—as they inevitably do—we experience distress. The practitioner, however, approaches these shifts with a mind established in equanimity.

This does not mean passivity. On the contrary, the highest form of strategic clarity arises when one understands the nature of change without being consumed by it. By recognizing the contingency of our projects, we can act with greater wisdom and less attachment. We engage with the world—innovating, negotiating, and leading—but we do so with the understanding that the results are not entirely within our hands. We cultivate a "non-clinging" approach to our work, where we are fully committed to the process, yet detached from the outcome. This is the path of the wise leader: to act with precision, to be fully present in the face of uncertainty, and to maintain an unshakable inner peace regardless of the external turbulence.

From the Samyutta Nikaya, the Buddha teaches on the nature of the world as a bubble, easily broken and lacking substance. SN 22.95: The Phenapinda Sutta.
