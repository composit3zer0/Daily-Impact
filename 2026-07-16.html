Dhamma Corner

In our current climate of rapid technological acceleration and geopolitical volatility, the mind naturally seeks refuge in certainty—in the "next big thing" or the "final strategic advantage." Yet, as we observe the shifting tides of markets and the rise of autonomous agents, we see the very definition of "security" being challenged. The Buddha taught that the world is inherently unstable, characterized by anicca (impermanence).

Leaders and innovators often operate under the illusion that by building more infrastructure—more chillers, more servers, more robust systems—they can create a static, safe harbor. However, the teaching of the Kaccanagotta Sutta reminds us that the world is generally caught between two extremes: the belief that things exist in a fixed, permanent way, and the belief that nothing matters because everything is annihilated. The Middle Way avoids these traps.

For the decision-maker, this means recognizing that technology is neither the savior nor the enemy. It is a process. When IBM falters or a new AI agent emerges, these are simply manifestations of changing conditions. The practice is to engage with these developments with a mind that is not "grasped" by them. If you tie your identity or your firm's existence to a specific technological paradigm, you will suffer when that paradigm shifts. True resilience is not found in the rigidity of one's systems, but in the flexibility of one's awareness. By acting with wisdom and non-attachment, one can navigate the "AI boom" or the "geopolitical shock" without being tossed about by the winds of hope and fear.

Source: Kaccanagotta Sutta (SN 12.15) — On the Middle Way regarding existence and non-existence.
Personal Reflection

We are currently obsessed with "agentic" tools—systems that can think, plan, and execute for us. We delegate our labor, our analysis, and increasingly, our decision-making to these proxies. It feels like liberation. You have more time, more capacity, more "leverage." Yet, in this outsourcing of the mundane, we risk outsourcing the very faculty that makes us human: the capacity to deliberate in the face of uncertainty. When you let an algorithm decide, you are not just saving time; you are potentially eroding your own discernment. Are you still the master of your choices, or are you becoming the assistant to your own creations?

*If you were stripped of your most powerful digital tools today, what would remain of your ability to lead?*
