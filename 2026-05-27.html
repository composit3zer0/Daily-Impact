Dhamma Corner

In the modern rush toward institutional adoption of technology and the frantic management of geopolitical risk, we often lose sight of the fundamental Buddhist teaching on the nature of "becoming" (bhava). We see leaders, investors, and innovators striving to secure their positions, to build more resilient systems, and to control the chaotic flow of global events. Yet, the Dhamma reminds us that this thirst for consolidation is precisely what fuels the cycle of suffering.

The Buddha taught that there are three types of becoming: sense-sphere becoming, form-sphere becoming, and formless becoming. Today's news reflects a world deeply entrenched in sense-sphere becoming—the desire to acquire, to protect, and to solidify existence through assets, power, and infrastructure. We see the ECB warning of market instability, and we see nations testing missiles; both are attempts to secure a "self" or a "state" in a world that is inherently unstable.

The practice for the digital nomad, the innovator, and the decision-maker is not to abandon the world, but to engage with it through the lens of non-clinging. When you build a platform or navigate a political crisis, observe the underlying urge to grasp at a permanent outcome. Recognize that the "instability" the financial world fears is simply the manifestation of anicca—impermanence. If we can see the rise and fall of markets, technology, and nations as natural phenomena rather than personal threats, we can act with a clarity that is impossible for those driven by fear or greed.

True resilience is not found in more sophisticated algorithms or deeper bunkers, but in a mind that is unshakeable because it has relinquished the need for things to be other than what they are. By investigating the nature of our ambition and the roots of our anxiety, we move from being architects of illusion to being stewards of a more compassionate reality.

Source: The Buddha discusses the cessation of becoming in the Kaccanagotta Sutta (SN 12.15), which clarifies the path between existence and non-existence.
