Dhamma Corner

In a world of rapidly evolving artificial intelligence and shifting geopolitical power, the mind is often pulled toward the extremes of excitement or fear. We see firms rebranding themselves to capture the 'AI' label, driven by the hunger for growth and the fear of obsolescence. This is the nature of the conditioned world: it is always in flux, always demanding our attention, and always promising a stability that it cannot ultimately provide.

The teaching of the Buddha directs us back to the root of these impulses. When we observe the frenetic pace of technological change, we are observing the manifestation of *sankhara*—the conditioned, compounded things that arise, persist, and pass away. Leaders and innovators often find themselves caught in the trap of clinging to these conditions, believing that the next breakthrough or the next strategic move will finally secure their position. Yet, as the Buddha taught, whatever is subject to arising is subject to cessation.

For those navigating the complexities of modern leadership, the practice is not to withdraw from the world, but to engage with it through the lens of *yoniso manasikara*—wise attention. This means seeing the AI agent, the market fluctuation, or the geopolitical crisis not as an ultimate reality, but as a process of causes and conditions. When you understand that these phenomena lack an inherent, unchanging essence, you become less susceptible to the 'AI washing' or the panic of market volatility. You can act with clarity and compassion, rather than reacting out of greed or aversion.

The Buddha emphasized the importance of *appamada*—heedfulness or diligence. This is not the anxiety-driven diligence of the corporate world, but a calm, sustained awareness that recognizes what is truly essential. In the midst of the 'G2 flux' and the rapid deployment of autonomous systems, the most strategic move is to maintain a mind that is unshakeable. By grounding your strategy in ethical clarity and the recognition of impermanence, you create a foundation that no market or machine can disrupt.

Personal Reflection

Today, you are bombarded with claims of 'intelligence' from machines and predictions of 'collapse' from geopolitical analysts. It is easy to feel that your value is tied to your ability to keep pace with these external, accelerating systems. But beneath the demand for your attention, there is a quieter, more fundamental question about what you are actually building. If you strip away the labels, the titles, and the technological tools, what is the core intention driving your choices? Are you running toward a horizon of your own choosing, or are you simply being carried by the current of others' ambitions?

If you knew that the tools you are currently obsessed with perfecting would be obsolete in five years, would your commitment to your current path remain the same?
