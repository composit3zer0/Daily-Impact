Dhamma Corner

In a world obsessed with "agentic" systems—autonomous programs designed to operate on our behalf—we see an extreme manifestation of the desire for control. We seek to build tools that act, decide, and optimize, hoping that by perfecting these external agents, we might finally secure our peace, our productivity, and our prosperity.

Yet, the Dhamma teaches us that this search for security through external mastery is a fundamental misunderstanding of the human condition. We are driven by the belief that if we can just arrange the conditions of our environment—the supply chains, the trade agreements, the AI infrastructure—then we will be free from the friction of existence. We treat the "self" as something that needs to be defended, enhanced, and immortalized through our creations.

The early Buddhist teachings remind us that all conditioned things are impermanent, unsatisfactory, and non-self. When we identify too closely with our projects, our companies, or our technological ambitions, we invite suffering. The "agent" is not the person; the "agent" is merely a set of processes, just as the self is a process of changing perceptions, feelings, and intentions. To cling to the idea that these technological agents are extensions of our own power is to mistake a shadow for the body.

For the decision-maker or the innovator, the challenge is not to stop building, but to build with the understanding of emptiness. When you create a system, observe how it arises due to specific conditions, and how it will inevitably pass away. By cultivating this awareness, you can engage with the world with full energy and precision, yet without the desperate grasping that leads to anxiety. You become the master of your actions, not the slave of your outcomes.

Reference: The Anatta-lakkhana Sutta (The Discourse on the Characteristic of Non-Self), SN 22.59
Personal Reflection

You are currently surrounded by an unprecedented volume of information and an ever-increasing pressure to "optimize" your life and your work. You are building systems to handle your tasks, automate your decisions, and secure your future. But in the midst of this architectural frenzy, it is easy to forget that the most important system you will ever manage is your own attention.

When you look at the news, the trends, and the shifting power structures of the world, ask yourself: are you observing these things to understand, or to distract yourself from the silence of your own presence?

If you were stripped of all the systems and tools you have built to define your success, who would you be in the quiet that remains?
