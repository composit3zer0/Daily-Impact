Dhamma Corner

In today's headlines, we witness the high-stakes dance of diplomacy and the volatile shifts of global markets. Leaders navigate the "war premium," attempting to manage systemic risks while projecting strength. From a Dhamma perspective, this is the classic manifestation of *anicca*—impermanence. The apparent solidity of a geopolitical alignment or a market trend is, in truth, a fleeting configuration of conditions, constantly arising and passing away.

The Buddha frequently taught that suffering—*dukkha*—is intimately tied to the craving for stability in a world that is inherently unstable. When leaders or investors grasp at "peace" or "growth" as if they are fixed, permanent states, they inevitably invite anxiety and disappointment. The teaching of the *Kaccanagotta Sutta* reminds us that the world is generally inclined toward two views: the view of existence and the view of non-existence. The wise person avoids these extremes, seeing the world as a process of dependent origination.

For the decision-maker or the innovator, this is not a call to passivity. On the contrary, it is an invitation to radical clarity. When you accept that the market, the conflict, and your own project are processes rather than fixed entities, you gain the ability to act with precision rather than reaction. You stop trying to "hold" the world in place and start working skillfully with the conditions as they present themselves.

The *Kaccanagotta Sutta* highlights that when one sees the arising and passing away of the world with right wisdom, there is no place for the self-centered fear that usually dictates policy and investment. You become the observer of the process, not the victim of it. In your next negotiation or strategic pivot, pause to see the "arising and passing away" of the variables in front of you. By letting go of the need for the situation to be permanent, you find the space to respond with the equanimity required for true leadership.

Kaccanagotta Sutta: To Kaccanagotta (SN 12.15) — On Right View and the Middle Way.
