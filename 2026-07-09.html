Dhamma Corner

In the current landscape of shifting geopolitical tides and the rapid, disruptive integration of artificial intelligence, leaders often find themselves grasping for control. We observe the markets reacting to strikes, trade fragmentation, and energy shocks—a volatile dance of cause and effect. Early Buddhist teaching offers a profound lens for this modern turbulence: the realization of *anicca*, or impermanence.

The global economy, much like the physical world, is a flux of interconnected conditions. When the IMF adjusts its forecasts, it is merely recognizing that the conditions for growth have changed. The suffering—or *dukkha*—that arises in the boardroom or the trading floor often stems from the desire for these conditions to be stable, predictable, and aligned with our expectations. When they are not, we experience the friction of resistance.

The practice for the modern innovator is not to withdraw from the world, but to engage with it while remaining unattached to the outcome. This is the essence of the "middle way." As leaders, we must navigate the AI revolution and the realities of war with clear, calm judgment. We use the tools available—the AI agents, the data, the logistics—but we do so with the understanding that these, too, are subject to change. They are not the source of our security.

By cultivating a mind that is steady in the face of both gain and loss, we become more effective, more compassionate, and more resilient. The ability to act decisively without being governed by the fear of loss—or the greed for excess—is the mark of true leadership in a fragmented world. When we see the world as it is, rather than as we wish it to be, we find the space to act with wisdom.

Reference: The *Sallatha Sutta*, SN 36.6, from the Samyutta Nikaya, which discusses the two darts of suffering: the physical event and our mental reaction to it. Read the Sallatha Sutta on SuttaCentral.

Yet, consider the space *between* the events. While the world demands your constant attention to the latest crisis, your capacity to choose your internal response remains untouched by the volatility of the markets or the decisions of nations. You are the observer of the change, not the change itself. In the quiet moments of your day, look past the headlines and ask yourself if your ambition is serving your purpose, or merely fueling your anxiety.
