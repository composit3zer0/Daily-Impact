Dhamma Corner

In a world where energy markets are weaponized and technological empires rise and fall based on quarterly projections, the mind often seeks refuge in the stability of the external. We look to the Strait of Hormuz, to the stock indices, to the legislative chambers of New York, hoping that by mastering these external conditions, we might finally find security.

Yet, the Buddha frequently reminded his followers that the world is inherently characterized by instability—anicca. To seek permanent satisfaction in the shifting patterns of the global economy is like trying to build a house on the surface of a river. The river flows, the water changes, and the house cannot hold its shape.

For the leader or the innovator, the practice is not to abandon the world, but to cultivate a mind that is not tethered to its fluctuations. When the market surges or the supply chain breaks, the uninstructed person feels these events as personal threats, leading to fear and impulsive action. The wise, however, observe these events as phenomena arising and passing away, much like the changing weather patterns now affecting Southeast Asia.

By recognizing that both the 'success' of an AI IPO and the 'failure' of a trade negotiation are conditioned phenomena, one gains a strategic clarity that the anxious mind cannot access. This is the Dhamma in action: not a withdrawal from responsibility, but the ability to act with compassion and precision, unburdened by the desperate need for a specific outcome.
