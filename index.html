Dhamma Corner

In the modern landscape of rapid technological shifts, geopolitical volatility, and the constant, frenetic movement of capital, we often feel as though we are standing on a precipice. We look to the news—the rise of AI, the shifting of borders, the disruption of supply chains—and we search for a ground to stand upon, a certainty that will survive the next cycle.

The Buddha taught that this search for external stability is, by its very nature, a search for that which cannot provide rest. In the *Sabba Sutta*, he explains that the world is defined by the "all"—the eye and forms, the ear and sounds, the mind and mental objects. We suffer because we attempt to define ourselves by these things, believing that if we can just master the technology, the market, or the geopolitical strategy, we will finally be secure.

However, the Dhamma points to a different path: the path of non-identification. For the decision-maker or the innovator, this does not mean detachment from action. It means engaging with the world with a clear understanding that the world is in a constant state of flux. When you invest, when you innovate, when you lead, do so with the clarity that the outcome is not under your absolute control. When you cease to demand that the world be static, you find a stillness that is not shaken by the *lokadhamma*—the eight worldly winds: gain and loss, status and disgrace, blame and praise, pleasure and pain.

The innovator who understands this is more effective, not less. By letting go of the desperate need for a specific result, the mind becomes lighter, more flexible, and more capable of seeing the reality of a situation without the distortion of fear or greed. You become like water, as the editorial roundtable noted—not because you are weak, but because you are no longer fighting against the nature of the current.

Return to the breath, return to the present moment, and recognize that the only true power you have is the power of your own attention. Use it well.

Source: SN 35.23: The All (Sabba Sutta)
Personal Reflection

Today’s news is filled with the language of "securing" futures—securing data, securing energy, securing borders, securing profit. It is a natural human instinct to build walls against the uncertainty that tomorrow inevitably brings. But as you navigate your own professional and personal ambitions today, consider what you are truly trying to protect.

Is it your status, your identity, or your peace of mind? We often confuse our plans for our lives with our lives themselves. When the plan shifts, we feel as though we are losing ourselves. What if you allowed the uncertainty to be part of the landscape, rather than an enemy to be defeated?

If you stripped away the need to control the outcome of your current project, what would you do differently today?
