Dhamma Corner

In the face of the sudden earthquake in the Philippines and the volatility of global markets, we are reminded of the fundamental truth of anicca—impermanence. We often construct our lives, our businesses, and our digital infrastructures with the assumption of stability, believing that if we just apply enough governance, enough foresight, or enough capital, we can secure ourselves against the shifting winds of fortune.

Yet, the teachings remind us that all conditioned things are subject to change. The desire to control the uncontrollable—whether it be the seismic plates of the earth or the chaotic emergence of artificial intelligence—is the very root of our suffering. When we identify too closely with our systems, our portfolios, or our status, we become brittle. We shatter when the ground shifts because we have forgotten that the ground was never fixed to begin with.

For the leader, the innovator, and the digital nomad, the path to true security is not found in building higher walls or more complex governance models, but in the cultivation of upekkhā—equanimity. This is not indifference; it is the capacity to remain fully present and clear-sighted while the world changes around us. By understanding that all phenomena are transient, we can engage with the world with greater intelligence and less fear. We act with precision, not out of a desperate need to hold on, but out of a clear understanding of what is necessary in this moment.

As you navigate the disruptions of today, reflect on the nature of your own attachments. When the "earthquake" of change hits your project or your plans, observe the immediate reaction of the mind. Does it tighten? Does it grasp? In that observation lies the freedom to respond with wisdom rather than reaction.
