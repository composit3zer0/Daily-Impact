Dhamma Corner

In a world characterized by shifting alliances, geopolitical tremors, and the rapid pace of technological disruption, the mind often seeks a static point of reference. We look for certainty in treaties, in market indices, or in the stability of our borders. Yet, the Buddha consistently pointed to the inherent instability of the conditioned world—a reality we call anicca, or impermanence.

When the news reports on the volatility of the Strait of Hormuz or the shifting ambitions of global powers, we are witnessing the inevitable unfolding of compounded things. To attach our sense of security to these external movements is to build upon sand. The teaching does not suggest withdrawal from the world, but rather a shift in our relationship to it. For the leader, the innovator, or the digital nomad, the practice is to engage with the world's complexity while maintaining an inner stillness that is not contingent on the outcome of a negotiation or the success of a project.

Consider the practice of upekkhā—equanimity. This is not indifference; it is the capacity to remain balanced amidst the eight worldly winds: gain and loss, fame and disrepute, praise and blame, pleasure and pain. When geopolitical winds shift, the person of wisdom observes these changes with clarity, recognizing them as the natural movement of conditions, rather than as a personal affront or a crisis that threatens their fundamental identity.

True resilience is found not in the ability to predict the future, but in the capacity to meet the present moment with clarity and compassion. By understanding that all things—whether they be empires, markets, or technologies—are in a state of flux, we liberate our energy from the anxiety of trying to hold onto the unholdable. We become more agile, more decisive, and ultimately more effective, because our actions are driven by insight rather than reaction.

Source: AN 8.6: The Eight Worldly Conditions
