Dhamma Corner

In a world defined by the rapid oscillation between technological triumph and geopolitical peril, it is easy to feel as though we are standing on shifting sand. We seek security in data centers, energy independence, and military strength, yet the news cycle reminds us daily that these constructs are inherently fragile. They are subject to the law of impermanence—the fundamental insight that what arises must also pass away.

The Buddha taught that our suffering often arises from the attempt to find lasting stability in that which is inherently unstable. When we identify our success solely with the expansion of a data hub or the dominance of an industry, we tether our inner peace to external, fluctuating conditions. The practice for the innovator and the leader is not to abandon the world, but to engage with it while maintaining the 'unshakable heart'—a mind that is not rattled by the successes or the collapses of the marketplace.

Consider the nature of the 'self' in the context of your ambition. If your identity is built on the outcome of your ventures, you will experience the rise and fall of your projects as personal crises. However, by cultivating mindfulness, you can observe these developments—the geopolitical tensions, the scientific breakthroughs—as phenomena arising and ceasing, without needing to grasp at them as the final word on your worth or your future.

True intelligence, in the Dhamma sense, is the clarity to see things exactly as they are: transient, interdependent, and ultimately not subject to our absolute control. By letting go of the need for the world to conform to our desires, we find a different kind of power—the power of equanimity. This allows for more strategic, compassionate, and wise decision-making, even in the midst of a volatile global landscape.

Ref: SN 35.1 — The Uninstructed Worldling (Samyutta Nikaya)
