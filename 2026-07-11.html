Dhamma Corner

In a world defined by rapid technological shifts, volatile energy markets, and the persistent desire to secure one's place in the global hierarchy, the mind often becomes a feverish instrument. We chase the "brighter" display, the "faster" synthesis, and the "higher" growth, believing that these outer achievements will finally settle the inner agitation. Yet, as the Buddha taught, the nature of the conditioned world is precisely this: it is always in flux, always subject to the rise and fall of conditions beyond our total control.

The recent research into the nature of time—revealing it as an emergent property of quantum relationships rather than a fixed, external ticking—mirrors the ancient insight that 'time' is a conceptual construction. We suffer because we cling to a fixed idea of how our lives, our markets, and our careers *should* unfold. When the actual conditions deviate from our projections, we experience the friction of resistance. True intelligence, for the leader or the innovator, lies in recognizing the impermanence of these conditions without retreating into despair or nihilism.

When the ADB forecasts a slowdown or geopolitical tensions disrupt supply chains, the wise person does not see a personal failure, but a shift in the currents of *paticcasamuppada* (dependent origination). Everything that arises, arises due to a complex web of causes. By understanding this, you can move through the world with the fluidity of water, as mentioned in our reflection—not by being passive, but by acting with a clear, unclouded mind that is not hijacked by the fear of loss or the grasping for more.

This is the practice of *sati* (mindfulness) applied to the boardroom and the laboratory. It is the ability to see the "mini universe" of your own project or portfolio, recognizing that it, too, is a dynamic system. When you release the demand that the system remain static or always favorable, you gain the clarity to respond effectively. You do not just manage; you harmonize.
