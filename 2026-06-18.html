Dhamma Corner

In today's headlines, we witness the great movements of the world—treaties signed, markets rallying, and the rapid, almost feverish acceleration of artificial intelligence. Leaders scramble to secure energy, and investors watch the "dots" of central bank policy with bated breath. It is a world of constant flux, where the desire for security is met only by the relentless nature of change.

The Buddha taught that this world is characterized by *anicca* (impermanence). When we look at the fluctuations in oil prices or the shifting alliances between nations, we are seeing the macro expression of this truth. The mistake, as the wise have always noted, is to assume that any of these structures—whether a government, a market, or a corporate strategy—can be held still. They are, by their very nature, processes in motion.

For the decision-maker, the innovator, and the digital nomad, there is profound freedom in recognizing this. If you are building a strategy based on the assumption of a static future, you are building on sand. Instead, the teaching of the *Kalamas* encourages us to test, to observe, and to rely on our own direct experience rather than clinging to the prevailing narratives of the day. When the Fed pivots or a geopolitical crisis abates, the skillful practitioner does not panic nor celebrate excessively. They observe the arising and passing away of these conditions with an equanimous mind.

True resilience is not found in the strength of one's fortress, but in the flexibility of one's mind. When you encounter the "AI boom" or the volatility of a market, ask yourself: "Am I clinging to the outcome, or am I engaging with the process?" The former leads to anxiety and the latter to clarity. By letting go of the need for the world to be different than it is, one finds the space to act with precision, compassion, and profound insight.

Reference: The Buddha's teaching on the nature of change and the path to peace in the Kalama Sutta (AN 3.65).

It is easy to feel that your identity is tied to the success or failure of these choices. But consider this: the person who makes the decision is not the same person who experiences the outcome. The landscape shifts, the data changes, and you evolve with it. Your value is not in the permanence of your plans, but in the clarity of your attention.
