Dhamma Corner

In a world defined by the rapid oscillation between geopolitical crisis and technological breakthrough, the mind often mirrors this volatility. We seek stability in external agreements—like the de-escalation talks between nations—or in the promise of new infrastructure, hoping these will finally provide the security we crave. Yet, the Buddha teaches that the world is inherently characterized by anicca, or impermanence. Seeking lasting security in conditioned things is akin to trying to grasp the wind; it is a movement that can never find a resting place.

The teaching on the "unconditioned" points to a different approach. When we are caught in the "AI arms race" or the anxiety of market fluctuations, we are identifying with the process of becoming. We are constantly striving to secure our position, our wealth, or our status, forgetting that these are all subject to change. The practice for a leader or an innovator is not to retreat from the world, but to engage with it while maintaining the "inner citadel" of awareness. By recognizing that the external events—whether a shift in monetary policy or a climate-driven supply shock—are merely passing phenomena, one can act with clarity rather than reaction.

Consider the practice of the four foundations of mindfulness. When you observe the market, observe also the mind that is watching the market. Is it grasping? Is it fearing? Is it clinging to an outcome? By bringing the same analytical rigor to our internal state as we do to our business strategies, we begin to see the gap between the event and our reaction. This gap is where freedom resides. It is not a passive withdrawal, but a profound, active presence that allows for skillfulness in action. We do not need the world to be perfect to be at peace; we need only to understand the nature of the world as it is.

Personal Reflection

You spend much of your day calculating the impact of external forces—markets, policies, innovations—on your future. You map out scenarios, hedge your risks, and build your fortresses. But in this constant projection toward what might happen, you often lose the only thing that is truly yours: the quality of your attention in this moment. When you strip away the ambition and the fear of what the next 24 hours might bring, what is the core of the identity that remains? Are you the architect of your plans, or are you the one watching the plans unfold?
