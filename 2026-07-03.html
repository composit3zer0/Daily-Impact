Dhamma Corner

In the face of today’s news—the sudden violence in Kyiv, the seismic shifts in Indonesia, and the frantic attempts to regulate the invisible currents of AI—it is easy to feel as though the ground beneath us is perpetually unstable. We seek to build cathedrals of certainty, only to find that the very foundation of our ambition is subject to the law of impermanence (anicca).

The early Buddhist teachings remind us that suffering (dukkha) arises not from the events of the world themselves, but from our desperate attempt to hold onto them, to make them permanent, or to bend them to our will. When we observe the political maneuvering or the market volatility, we see individuals and institutions grasping at shadows. They are trying to create a static reality in a stream that never stops flowing.

For the leader or the innovator, the Dhamma does not suggest withdrawal. Rather, it suggests a profound shift in perspective. To act effectively in a world of constant change, one must act without the heavy burden of attachment to the outcome. When you work on your next project, or navigate your next negotiation, can you do so with full presence, while remaining unattached to the "dent" you wish to leave on the universe? This is the path of the middle way—where one is neither passive nor obsessed, but fully engaged with the reality of the present moment.

The Buddha taught the importance of mindfulness and clear comprehension in every action. Whether you are managing a crisis or launching a new technology, the clarity of your intent matters more than the magnitude of your influence. When the storm comes, the tree that bends survives; the tree that resists is broken. By cultivating this inner fluidity, you become a source of stability for those around you, not because you have mastered the world, but because you have mastered your own response to it.
