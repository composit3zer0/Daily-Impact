Dhamma Corner

In a world of rapid technological advancement and political maneuvering, where states seek to own the very intelligence that drives our future, the mind is often caught in a whirlwind of acquisition and protection. We see the desire for equity and ownership—the instinct to secure a stake in the "next big thing"—as a means of ensuring stability. Yet, the Dhamma teaches that this very impulse is rooted in the delusion of a permanent self that can be fortified against the currents of change.

When the mind clings to the idea that it can own, control, or possess the future, it creates a friction that inevitably leads to suffering. The Buddha consistently pointed to the nature of anicca, or impermanence. Everything we touch, every structure we build, and every AI model we deploy is subject to the same law of arising and passing away. To believe that we can anchor our identity or our security in these external, shifting arrangements is to grasp at water.

For the decision-maker or the innovator, the practice is not to abandon the tools of the world, but to engage with them without the heavy burden of attachment. It is to work with skill and precision, as a master artisan works with wood, knowing that the wood itself is subject to decay. By recognizing the empty nature of these corporate and state structures, one finds a clarity that allows for more effective, compassionate action. You are not the owner of the technology; you are a temporary steward of a process.

True intelligence is not found in the acquisition of assets, but in the capacity to see clearly. When you can observe the rise and fall of market trends, the shifting of geopolitical power, and the evolution of machines without being swept away by fear or greed, you operate from a place of profound stability. This is the "unshakeable" quality of a mind that has realized the truth of non-attachment.

You spend your days building, investing, and navigating systems designed to last. You are constantly asked to define your value by what you own or what you can control. But consider the moments when you are truly at peace—are they found in the accumulation of stakes and assets, or in the quiet realization that you are merely the observer of all these things? Your ambition is a powerful engine, but it requires a steering hand that is not afraid of letting go. When the structures you serve begin to shift, where do you find your footing?
