Dhamma Corner

In a world of rapidly shifting geopolitical alliances, market disruptions, and the relentless evolution of artificial intelligence, the mind is often pulled into a state of reactive agitation. We seek stability in external conditions—a peace treaty, a stock price, or a new technological edge—believing that if we can just manage the world correctly, we will finally be secure.

The Buddha taught that this reliance on external conditions is the root of our dissatisfaction. In the Sallatha Sutta, the Buddha describes the experience of the uninstructed person who, when faced with painful feelings or uncertainty, reacts with sorrow, lamentation, and desperation. They are like a person struck by two arrows: the first, the inevitable change of the world (the physical event), and the second, the internal reaction of craving, aversion, and grasping (the psychological event).

For the modern leader or innovator, the first arrow is unavoidable. Markets will fluctuate; tech will displace; empires will negotiate. However, the second arrow—the mental anguish caused by our resistance to these changes—is optional. When you observe the "two-track" labour market or the complexities of global supply chains, you have a choice: to be consumed by the fear of obsolescence or the desire for control, or to observe these phenomena with a clarity that is not swayed by them.

True resilience is not found in building a higher wall or a better algorithm, but in the cultivation of upekkhā, or equanimity. This is not indifference. It is the ability to engage fully with the world while maintaining a center that is not dependent on the outcome. When you work, work with excellence. When you lead, lead with compassion. But do not let your identity or your inner peace be tethered to the fluctuating winds of the global economy. By releasing the second arrow, you become more effective, for you are no longer blinded by the frantic need to grasp at what is, by its very nature, impermanent.

Source: Sallatha Sutta: The Dart (SN 36.6)
