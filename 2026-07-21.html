Dhamma Corner

In a world defined by rapid technological acceleration and geopolitical volatility, the mind often seeks a fixed point of stability. We see leaders and markets reacting to external shocks—oil prices rising, AI compute capacity straining, and maritime borders hardening. These are the winds of the world, shifting constantly as they have since the dawn of time.

The teaching of the Buddha emphasizes the nature of anicca, or impermanence. When we cling to the idea that a specific geopolitical order or a particular economic boom must remain stable, we invite suffering. The discomfort felt by decision-makers today arises from this very friction: the expectation of continuity in a world governed by change.

For the innovator or the leader, the Dhamma does not suggest retreat. Rather, it suggests a profound shift in orientation. Instead of attempting to control the external storm—which is beyond any single person's grasp—one cultivates a mind that is suñña, or empty of the grasping that creates anxiety. When you recognize that both the prosperity of the AI sector and the volatility of energy markets are dependently arisen phenomena, you are no longer a victim of their fluctuations. You act with clarity, not from a place of reactive fear, but from a place of responsive wisdom.

As noted in the Sabbasava Sutta, the wise person is one who understands the origin of the fermentation of the mind—the clinging to "I" and "mine"—and learns to let go of these unskillful states. By applying this to your professional life, you retain the ability to innovate and lead without being consumed by the inevitable shifts of the global landscape.

Reference: Sabbasava Sutta: All the Taints (MN 2)
