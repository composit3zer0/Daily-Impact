Dhamma Corner

In the midst of shifting geopolitical alliances and the rapid evolution of global markets, the mind often seeks a stable ground—a "final" peace deal, a "secure" investment, or a "certain" future. Yet, the early teachings of the Buddha consistently remind us that such stability is not found in the external world of conditioned things, but in the understanding of the nature of that very world.

The Satipatthana Sutta emphasizes the practice of mindfulness as a way to see things as they are, rather than as we wish them to be. When leaders and innovators focus on the "next big thing" or the "final deal," they are often caught in the trap of clinging to a future that is inherently impermanent. By applying this same keen observation to our strategic decisions, we can cultivate a clarity that is not shaken by the rise and fall of market indices or the volatility of international relations.

This is not a call for passivity, but for an active, detached engagement. To act with "right effort" is to pursue one's work with dedication while simultaneously recognizing that the outcome is subject to conditions beyond one's control. When we realize the impermanent nature of all institutions and agreements, we act with greater skill, less desperation, and more profound effectiveness. We stop trying to hold onto the water, as Bruce Lee might suggest, and instead move with the current, fully present to the reality of the moment.

For the decision-maker, this means making the hard call without the emotional weight of fear or the blinding light of greed. It is the practice of "wise attention"—seeing the risk, seeing the opportunity, and seeing the impermanence of both. By grounding oneself in this understanding, one becomes a more resilient navigator of the global change that defines our time.
