Dhamma Corner

In a world defined by the rapid, often volatile movement of markets, geopolitical shifts, and the relentless pursuit of technological growth, it is easy to feel as though one is standing on shifting sands. We see leaders trading threats and markets swinging on the whims of global tension. Yet, the early teachings of the Buddha invite us to look closer at the nature of this volatility itself.

The Buddha frequently taught the concept of anicca, or impermanence. Everything conditioned—whether it be the stock of a technology company, the stability of a regional trade bloc, or the power of a sovereign state—is subject to change. The suffering that arises for the investor or the innovator often stems not from the change itself, but from the desperate grasping for a permanence that does not exist. We act as if we can fix the world in place, yet the very nature of existence is a constant, flowing flux.

For those navigating the complexities of 2026, the practice is not to ignore these developments but to engage with them from a position of inner clarity. When the news cycles bring anxiety, notice the craving for a different reality—a desire for the market to be stable, for the conflict to be resolved, for the technology to be perfectly predictable. This craving is the source of the agitation. By recognizing that these events are unfolding according to their own causes and conditions, one can act with strategic intelligence rather than reactive fear. This is the difference between being tossed by the waves and learning to navigate the tide.

Source: Anattalakkhana Sutta (SN 22.59) — The Discourse on the Not-Self Characteristic
