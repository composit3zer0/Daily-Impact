Dhamma Corner

In the modern arena of high-stakes strategy, the concept of "security" is often treated as the ultimate goal—a fortress built of energy contracts, supply chains, and technological moats. Yet, the Buddha taught that the seeking of security in external forms is akin to grasping at smoke. The world is characterized by anicca, or impermanence; the very systems we build to ensure our safety are, by their nature, subject to decay and transformation.

Leaders and innovators often suffer from the delusion that they can engineer a permanent state of stability. However, the Sutta Pitaka reminds us that the only true refuge is found in the development of the mind—specifically, in the cultivation of wisdom and the detachment from the outcomes of our worldly ambitions. When you become overly identified with the "moats" of your industry, you become vulnerable to the shifts in the tide that you cannot control.

The practice is not to abandon your responsibilities or your work, but to perform them with a mind that understands their conditioned nature. By acknowledging that all formations—whether they be AI companies, energy grids, or political alliances—are in a constant state of flux, you can navigate them with a sense of equanimity. This is not passive resignation; it is the highest form of strategic intelligence. It allows you to move with the changes rather than being shattered by them, as a tree that bends in the wind survives, while the rigid one snaps.

As you manage your enterprises today, consider the nature of your attachment to the "bottlenecks" and "security measures" you pursue. Are they truly serving a higher purpose, or are they feeding the anxiety of an unquiet mind? Cultivate the clarity to see the difference.

Reference: The nature of conditioned things and the path to liberation, SN 22.90 — The Burden Sutta.
