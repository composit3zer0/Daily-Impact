Dhamma Corner

In the current landscape of global power, we witness a frantic scramble for ownership—governments seeking stakes in AI, corporations pivoting to capture infrastructure, and nations competing for industrial dominance. This pursuit of control, while seemingly pragmatic, often masks a deeper, pervasive anxiety: the fear that if we do not possess and regulate, we will be left behind by the relentless tide of change.

The Buddha taught that the root of such distress is *upādāna*, or clinging. We cling to the idea of the "frontier," to the status of a market leader, and to the safety of state-backed security. Yet, as the Dhamma reminds us, all conditioned things are impermanent (*anicca*). The very infrastructure we build today, the models we fight to own, and the alliances we forge are subject to the same laws of decay and transformation as any other phenomenon.

For the leader or innovator, the challenge is not to abandon the world of action, but to engage with it without the suffocating grip of attachment. When we act from a place of *upādāna*, our decisions become reactive, driven by fear and the desire to preserve a self-image that is inherently unstable. When we act from a place of clarity, we can see the "water" that Bruce Lee speaks of—the fluid nature of reality—and navigate it with skill, rather than trying to force it into a static, rigid shape.

True resilience in this era of "civilizational" shifts comes not from accumulating more influence, but from cultivating a mind that is unshakeable, regardless of whether the market rises or falls, or whether our strategic bets succeed or fail. By letting go of the need to possess the outcome, we gain the freedom to act with genuine wisdom and compassion.

Personal Reflection

You spend your days building systems, securing assets, and positioning yourself for a future that feels increasingly precarious. It is easy to view your life as a series of investments—time, capital, and attention—that must yield a return. But consider the moments when you are most alive: they are rarely when you are holding onto control, but when you are fully present with the work itself, unburdened by the outcome. If you were to stop trying to own the future, what would you be free to create today?

What are you holding onto that is preventing you from seeing the present moment clearly?
