Dhamma Corner

The world is currently in a state of rapid, often violent, reconfiguration. From the closure of critical maritime chokepoints to the breakdown of diplomatic agreements, the external environment reflects the instability of the human mind. We observe leaders and nations clinging to positions, reacting with force, and attempting to secure their interests through the very actions that guarantee further suffering.

In the early teachings, the Buddha often spoke of the "world" not merely as a physical place, but as a construct of our perceptions, attachments, and reactions (Sabba Sutta). When we perceive a threat—whether to our supply chains, our borders, or our ideologies—we immediately enter a cycle of craving and aversion. We crave security and stability; we have aversion toward disruption and loss. This cycle, known as *paticcasamuppada* (dependent origination), is the mechanism that drives the volatility we see in today's headlines.

For the decision-maker or innovator, the challenge is to act in the world without being enslaved by the world. This requires *sati* (mindfulness) and *panna* (wisdom). Mindfulness allows you to observe the geopolitical turbulence without being swept away by the reactive currents of fear or aggression. Wisdom enables you to see that these structures are *anicca* (impermanent). When one recognizes that all conditions are in flux, one stops trying to force a static outcome upon a dynamic reality.

True resilience is not found in the strength of a blockade or the power of a military, but in the spaciousness of a mind that is not rattled by the shifting tides. By cultivating this internal stillness, you become a stabilizing force in your own sphere of influence. You act not out of a desperate need to hold on, but out of a clear understanding of what is necessary and beneficial for the long term. As the Buddha taught, it is by letting go of the need to control the uncontrollable that one finds the true path to peace.

Source: SN 35.23: The All (Sabba Sutta)
