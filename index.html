Dhamma Corner

In the rapid flux of 2026—where tech giants shed thousands of workers to chase the phantom of AI dominance and geopolitical tremors threaten the very arteries of global trade—it is easy to feel that we are losing our footing. We see the "structural resilience" of markets tested against the reality of depleted reserves, much like a person attempting to live on a diminishing inheritance.

The Buddha taught that the world is in constant transformation—*anicca*, or impermanence. The distress felt by the modern leader, investor, or nomad is often rooted in the craving for a stability that the conditioned world cannot provide. We build arches and empires, hoping they will stand against the winds of change, yet the very act of grasping for permanence creates the suffering we seek to avoid.

In the *Kaccayanagotta Sutta*, the Buddha explains that the world generally relies on a duality of "it is" and "it is not." People are bound by their views and attachments. When we view the world as a place to be secured and controlled, we are inevitably disappointed when it shifts. The practice for the decision-maker is not to retreat from the world, but to engage with it without the suffocating grip of attachment. To see the rise and fall of markets, the shifts in power, and the disruptions of technology as they are—as processes, not as personal affronts or final losses.

By cultivating *upekkhā* (equanimity), one can navigate the volatility of the Strait of Hormuz or the boardroom with a clear, steady mind. This is not indifference; it is the highest form of strategic intelligence. It allows for agile action because the mind is not clouded by the fear of loss or the frantic desire for gain. When you recognize that the "self" and the "company" are not solid, independent entities but interconnected, changing processes, you can act with precision and compassion.

As you move through your day, observe the urge to control the outcome. Notice how that urge creates tension. Then, gently release it. Act with excellence, but rest in the understanding that the results are beyond your ultimate control.

Reference: Kaccayanagotta Sutta: To Kaccayana Gotta (SN 12.15)
Personal Reflection

Today, the news cycle is dominated by the tension between aggressive growth and the fragility of our systems—whether it is the layoffs at Microsoft or the geopolitical brinkmanship in the Middle East. It is tempting to define yourself by your capacity to manage this chaos, to be the one who stays afloat while others sink. But consider the cost of that identity. When you equate your value with your ability to navigate volatility, you become a prisoner to the very chaos you are trying to master.

What part of your ambition is driven by a genuine desire to create, and what part is merely a defense mechanism against the fear of becoming irrelevant?
