Dhamma Corner

In a world defined by rapid technological leaps and the unsettling volatility of geopolitical conflict, the mind is often pulled into a state of constant reaction. We see the markets shift, the strikes in the Strait of Hormuz, and the frantic race for artificial intelligence supremacy, and we feel a compulsion to grasp at certainty. Yet, the Buddha’s teaching consistently points to the nature of anicca—impermanence. The instability in our external systems is not a flaw in the system; it is the system.

For the decision-maker or the innovator, the challenge is not to fix the world so that it becomes permanent, but to develop the clarity to see it as it is. When the Fed signals concern or a new model is launched, we are witnessing the unfolding of dependent origination. One event gives rise to another in a chain that has no discernible beginning. To remain serene amidst this is not to be indifferent, but to be fully present without being swept away by the narrative of "my market" or "my stability."

The practice is to cultivate a steady, grounded awareness that does not retreat from the complexity of the world but observes it with equanimity. By recognizing that all compounded things are subject to change, we move from a state of reactive anxiety to one of responsive intelligence. This is the difference between being a slave to the news cycle and being a master of your attention. When you can witness the rise and fall of empires, markets, and models with the same calm observation, you are no longer a victim of the unfolding world, but an active, clear-eyed participant in it.

Source: SN 35.1 — The All (Sabba Sutta)
Personal Reflection

You spend your days navigating the currents of global events, calculating risks, and managing the output of machines. It is easy to confuse your identity with the outcomes of these systems—your portfolio, your projects, your influence. But when the noise settles and the screens go dark, what remains of you that is not defined by your utility or your concerns? You are not the events you witness, nor the anxieties they produce. You are the space in which they appear.

If you were stripped of your current role and the problems you are trying to solve, who would you be when the world is no longer asking for your attention?
