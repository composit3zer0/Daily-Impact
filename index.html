Dhamma Corner

In a world where nations vie for control over waterways and technological infrastructure, we observe a pervasive struggle for security. The headlines today—the strikes in the Middle East, the diplomatic tension in ASEAN—are classic manifestations of the 'clinging to views' and 'clinging to possessions' that the Buddha identified as the root of conflict.

Leaders and innovators often operate under the assumption that if they can only secure the right resources, pass the right policy, or build the right system, they will achieve a state of lasting safety. Yet, the Dhamma teaches that all compounded things are subject to change. The Strait of Hormuz will always be a point of tension precisely because it is a condition of the world to be in flux. To cling to it as 'mine' or 'ours' is to invite the very suffering one seeks to avoid.

For the decision-maker, the path is not to abandon action, but to act without the heavy burden of grasping. When you negotiate, when you invest, or when you steer a team, can you do so with the clarity that the outcome is not yours to fully control? This is not passivity; it is the ultimate form of strategic agility. By seeing the impermanent nature of the market and the political landscape, you become less a victim of their volatility and more a steady presence within it.

True security is found not in the fortification of borders or the dominance of technology, but in the cultivation of a mind that is unshakeable, regardless of external conditions. As the Buddha noted, one who is grounded in the Dhamma does not tremble when the world shakes.
