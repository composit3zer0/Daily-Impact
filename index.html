Dhamma Corner

In a world defined by the turbulence of shifting supply chains, geopolitical brinkmanship, and the relentless speed of technological advancement, it is easy to feel as though one is standing on shifting sand. Today’s news highlights a global anxiety: we are reaching for more silicon, more alliances, and more control to secure our footing. Yet, the Dhamma teaches us that this search for security in external conditions is fundamentally flawed.

The Buddha frequently pointed to the nature of the world as anicca—impermanent and subject to change. When we attempt to find lasting stability in the fluctuations of the Strait of Hormuz or the volatility of the tech markets, we are trying to grasp the wind. The desire for a "secure" outcome is precisely what creates the suffering of the executive or the investor; it is the friction between how we want the world to be and how it actually manifests.

In the Sallatha Sutta, the Buddha illustrates this through the metaphor of the two darts. The first dart is the inevitable change of the world—the market volatility, the conflict, the disruption. This is the nature of existence. The second dart is our reaction to it: our fear, our grasping for safety, our anger at the disruption. We cannot always stop the first dart, but the second dart—the internal suffering—is entirely within our domain to prevent.

For those navigating the current era of change, the invitation is not to abandon strategy or innovation, but to detach the sense of self from the outcome. When you work, work with precision and clarity, but recognize that the results are subject to conditions beyond your control. By practicing this non-attachment, you maintain the mental capacity to remain "formless," as Bruce Lee suggested, adapting to the shifts without being broken by them. True leadership in this age is the ability to act decisively while remaining internally unmoved by the inevitable rise and fall of the world’s fortunes.

Reference: Sallatha Sutta: The Dart (SN 36.6)
Personal Reflection

You spend much of your day managing external variables—market signals, team dynamics, geopolitical risks. You are constantly building a structure of certainty to shield yourself from the unknown. Yet, notice how the more you tighten your grip on these external outcomes, the more fragile your inner state becomes. The world will continue to fluctuate regardless of your preparation. The question is not how much more you can control, but how much of your identity is currently tied to the success or failure of your projects.

*If the outcome you are working toward today were stripped of all its external rewards, would you still find meaning in the work itself?*
