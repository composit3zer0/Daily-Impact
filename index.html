Dhamma Corner

In the rapid flux of 2026—where the focus shifts from the abstract software of AI to the physical systems of power grids and resource extraction—the mind often races to keep pace. We are told that our survival and success depend on our ability to adapt to these shifting external landscapes. Yet, the Buddha teaches that the turmoil we perceive in the world is inextricably linked to the turmoil within our own perceptions.

The "everything-to-grid" energy model, the geopolitical maneuvering of nations, and the instability of political leadership are all expressions of *anicca* (impermanence). We attempt to build structures—political, economic, and technological—that are meant to endure, yet the very nature of these systems is to change, to break, and to be replaced. When we invest our identity in the success of a technology or the stability of a state, we become vulnerable to the inevitable fluctuations of those things.

For the leader or innovator, the Dhamma does not suggest withdrawing from the world or abandoning innovation. Instead, it invites a radical shift in perspective. It is possible to engage with the world—to build, to lead, to innovate—without the clinging that leads to suffering. This is the practice of "acting without attachment." When a decision is made not out of fear of loss or craving for gain, but with a clear, calm understanding of the present reality, that action becomes far more potent and effective.

Consider the *Sabbasava Sutta*, which instructs on how to manage the "influxes" or mental fermentations that arise when we encounter the world. By recognizing that these external events are not "me" or "mine," the mind remains unshakeable. Whether the markets rise or fall, or a new technology transforms our industry, the practitioner remains rooted in the stillness of the present. This is the ultimate competitive advantage: a mind that is not disrupted by the very volatility it seeks to navigate.
