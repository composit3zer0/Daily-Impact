Dhamma Corner

In the modern world of high-value decision-making, we often view the "geopolitical shock" or the "market disruption" as an external adversary—a force to be managed, defeated, or outwitted. Yet, the early teachings of the Buddha invite us to look closer at the nature of these phenomena. We see the world in a state of flux: oil supplies are volatile, cities face unprecedented heat, and national security strategies are constantly being renegotiated. This is the essence of anicca, or impermanence.

Leaders and innovators often suffer because they cling to the idea of a stable, predictable outcome. When the "next-gen AI server rack" is delayed or the "transatlantic contract" is shifted, the suffering arises not from the event itself, but from the refusal to accept that the conditions leading to those outcomes were never under our absolute control. The Buddha taught that clinging to things that are inherently subject to change is the root of dukkha, or dissatisfaction.

For the digital nomad, the investor, and the policymaker, the practice is not to abandon strategy, but to adopt a strategy of non-attachment. This does not mean passivity. It means engaging with the world with total clarity, putting forth your best effort, and then letting go of the outcome. When you work from a place of upekkhā—equanimity—you remain effective in the face of volatility because your mind is not clouded by the fear of loss or the grasping for gain. You see the geopolitical landscape not as a static board to be conquered, but as a shifting river of conditions. You navigate it with wisdom, recognizing that the only true security is the development of a mind that is not shaken by the changing winds of the world.

Reference: The Buddha's teaching on the nature of change and the path to peace. SN 35.1: The All (Sabba Sutta)
Personal Reflection

Today’s news is filled with talk of sovereignty, production targets, and global risk. It is easy to feel that your own agency is small against such vast, shifting currents. Yet, you are the one who chooses where to direct your attention. When you consume the headlines, do you look for ways to protect what you have, or for ways to understand the unfolding reality? We often confuse our ambition with our identity, fearing that if the market shifts, we are diminished. But you are not the market. You are the observer of the market.

If the structures you have built were to shift tomorrow, would you still know who you are?
