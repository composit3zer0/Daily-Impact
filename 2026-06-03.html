Dhamma Corner

In a world defined by rapid cabinet shuffles, technological pivots, and the looming uncertainty of climate shifts, the mind often seeks refuge in the illusion of control. We believe that if we can just optimize the chip, secure the trade route, or reorganize the department, we will achieve a state of lasting stability. Yet, the Dhamma teaches us that this search for permanence in a conditioned world is the very source of our unrest.

The Buddha often pointed to the nature of "sankhara"—the conditioned processes that make up our world and our experience. These processes are inherently unstable, subject to rise and fall, and ultimately beyond our command. When we identify with the success of our initiatives or the fear of our failures, we tether our inner peace to external events that are, by nature, shifting like the monsoon winds.

For the leader, the investor, and the innovator, the challenge is not to abandon these worldly responsibilities, but to engage with them through the lens of "yoniso manasikara"—wise attention. This means seeing the volatility of the market not as a personal threat, but as the natural expression of impermanence (anicca). When you recognize that the structures you build are as ephemeral as the cloud formations in an El Nino season, you are freed from the desperate need for them to be static.

True intelligence—the kind that serves a high-value decision-maker—is found in the ability to act with absolute clarity while remaining unattached to the outcome. By letting go of the craving for a "perfect" system, you gain the agility to respond to the reality of the present moment, rather than the phantom of what you wish it to be. This is the path of the steady mind: participating fully in the complexity of the world, yet remaining untouched by its turbulence.

Personal Reflection

Today, you are bombarded with signals of change—governments shifting, technologies evolving, and the climate itself turning. It is easy to feel that your identity is tied to the success of your latest project or the stability of your environment. But notice the part of you that is *observing* these changes. That observer is not the one losing a job, nor is it the one building a chip. It is the silent space in which all this occurs.

If your current ambition were to vanish tomorrow, what would remain of you that is truly essential?
