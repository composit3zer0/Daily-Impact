Dhamma Corner

In a world marked by rapid, often volatile, shifts in geopolitical power and technological dominance, the mind frequently seeks refuge in the stability of "our" side, "our" nation, or "our" success. We celebrate milestones, like the 250th anniversary of a nation, with a sense of permanence. Yet, the Dhamma teaches us that all compounded things are subject to the law of impermanence (anicca). The structures we build—be they trade agreements, data centers, or sovereign states—are inherently unstable, shifting like the tides of the South China Sea.

The Buddha frequently reminded his followers that attachment to these shifting configurations is the primary cause of suffering. When we identify too deeply with the success or failure of a system, we lose the clarity required to navigate it. The "geopolitical friction" described in today's news is simply the friction of competing desires and clashing views, a process as natural as the weather patterns that bring both storms and sunshine. We cannot control the external storm, but we can regulate the inner climate.

For the decision-maker, the investor, or the innovator, this is not an invitation to passivity. It is an invitation to radical objectivity. By recognizing the transient nature of the "new world order," one can act with greater agility. When you realize that the system is not a permanent pillar but a flowing process, you become less susceptible to the fear of loss and the intoxication of gain. True security is not found in the strength of an alliance or the efficiency of a supply chain, but in the unshakeable awareness of the present moment.

As the Buddha taught in the Sabbasava Sutta, the wise person is one who understands the nature of the defilements and how they arise through clinging to what is essentially hollow. By letting go of the need for these external structures to remain static, one finds a stillness that no market volatility can disturb. This is the ultimate form of strategic resilience.

Reference: MN 2: Sabbasava Sutta — The Discourse on All the Taints
