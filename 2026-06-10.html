Dhamma Corner

In our current era of rapid technological change and geopolitical volatility, the human tendency is to seek refuge in the illusion of control. We build larger buffers, develop more complex AI agents, and seek the security of consolidated energy assets. Yet, the early teachings of the Buddha remind us that the nature of the world is inherently unstable. The Pali term anicca, or impermanence, is not a pessimistic observation; it is a strategic reality. When we attempt to impose permanence on a system that is by nature in flux, we create the very suffering—or dukkha—that undermines our decision-making.

For the leader, the innovator, or the investor, the practice is not to abandon the tools of the world, but to engage with them without the grasping mind. When we see the "double exposure" of climate and market volatility facing farmers, we see the externalization of anicca. The wise response is not to despair, but to develop upekkhā, or equanimity. This is not indifference; it is the capacity to remain centered amidst the shifting tides of the global economy. By cultivating this stillness, one can observe the patterns of the market and the geopolitical landscape with greater clarity, allowing for decisions that are responsive rather than reactive.

As the Cūḷasaccaka Sutta suggests, the realization of the nature of phenomena—that they are not self, not permanent, and not reliable—is the beginning of true wisdom. When you understand that your portfolio, your enterprise, and your political structures are subject to the same laws of arising and passing away as the clouds in the sky, you become less a victim of circumstance and more a participant in the unfolding of reality. This is the ultimate competitive advantage: the mind that is not rattled by change is the mind that can best navigate it.

Reference: Cūḷasaccaka Sutta (MN 35) — The Shorter Discourse to Saccaka
