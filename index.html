Dhamma Corner

In the rapid flux of global events—the closing of straits, the migration of talent, the rise of powerful algorithms—we often feel as though we are standing on shifting sand. The teachings of the Buddha remind us that this instability is not an aberration; it is the fundamental nature of conditioned existence, known as anicca, or impermanence.

When leaders or innovators seek to build "secure" systems—whether it be an energy grid or a digital network—they are often working against the grain of reality. The desire for absolute control, for the perfect, unassailable position, is the root of dukkha, or suffering. When the market shifts, or a geopolitical deal collapses, the suffering arises not from the event itself, but from the clinging to an expectation that things should remain stable.

For the decision-maker, this is not a call to apathy or inaction. Rather, it is a call to "skilful action" (kusala kamma). When you act with a mind that understands impermanence, you act without the frantic energy of grasping. You build the data center, you negotiate the treaty, but you do so with the awareness that these structures are temporary. You become like the lotus that rests on the water, moving with the current without being submerged by it.

In the Kaccayanagotta Sutta, the Buddha provides a profound framework for navigating extremes. He speaks of avoiding the extremes of "existence" (clinging to the idea that things have permanent essence) and "non-existence" (nihilism). By seeing the arising and passing away of all phenomena, one walks the Middle Way. When you see the world as a series of interconnected, changing processes, you are no longer a victim of the "market shocks" or "geopolitical turbulence." You remain the observer, capable of making clear, compassionate decisions.
