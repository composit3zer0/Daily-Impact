Dhamma Corner

In the digital age, we are often overwhelmed by the sheer velocity of information—the record-breaking market volumes, the rapid scaling of AI infrastructure, and the cascading geopolitical shifts. The mind, grasping for stability, attempts to categorize these events, hoping to find a solid ground upon which to stand. Yet, the Dhamma teaches us that this search for permanence in the conditioned world is the primary source of our agitation.

The Buddha frequently reminded his followers that all compounded things are subject to change. Whether it is the rise of a new technological paradigm or the ebb and flow of regional power, these are all *sankhara*—conditioned phenomena. When we identify too closely with our success in these arenas, or when we become fearful of their decline, we lose the equanimity that allows for clear, strategic decision-making.

Consider the market—a vast, collective expression of craving and aversion. For the leader or the innovator, the challenge is not to avoid the market, but to engage with it without being consumed by it. This is the practice of *sati*, or mindfulness. By maintaining awareness of the impermanent nature of these digital and geopolitical landscapes, one can act with precision and detachment. You do not need to be indifferent to the outcome, but you must be free from the anxiety of the outcome.

As the *Cula-Malunkyovada Sutta* suggests, we are often like a man struck by a poisoned arrow who refuses to have it removed until he knows the name, caste, and origin of the archer. We demand to know the "why" of the market or the "who" behind the geopolitical move, while the immediate need is to address the suffering caused by our own clinging to these narratives. Focus on the immediate task—the skillful action in the present moment—and let the conditioned world revolve as it will.

Reference: Cūḷamālukya Sutta: The Shorter Instructions to Mālukya (MN 63)

It is easy to feel small, or conversely, to inflate your identity to match the scale of the systems you build or navigate. Yet, at the end of the day, the magnitude of the world does not alter the scale of your own consciousness. You are the space in which these events arise and pass away.
