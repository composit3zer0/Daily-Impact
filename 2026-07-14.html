Dhamma Corner

In the rapid flux of 2026, where geopolitics and artificial intelligence create a landscape of constant, shifting uncertainty, the teachings of the Buddha offer a profound anchor. We see leaders and markets reacting to external stimuli—blockades, debt, technological disruption—with frantic activity, hoping that more control, more capital, and more computation will provide stability. Yet, the Dhamma reminds us that this thirst for control is a pursuit of the ungraspable.

The teaching of Anicca (impermanence) is not a theoretical abstraction; it is the fundamental reality of the supply chains, the stock markets, and the political alliances we navigate daily. When we attempt to build a permanent structure—a monopoly, a secure route, or a dominant AI model—we are working against the nature of things. The suffering (Dukkha) arises not from the events themselves, but from our internal demand that the world remain fixed according to our desires.

For the decision-maker or innovator, the practice is not to abandon ambition, but to transform the quality of your attention. Can you lead with a mind that is fully engaged, yet not clinging to outcomes? This is the path of the "wise traveler." When the Strait of Hormuz is blocked or the tech sector faces a correction, the mind that is anchored in the present moment can respond with clarity rather than panic. You act because action is required, not because you are enslaved by the fear of loss or the hunger for gain.

In the Sabbasava Sutta, the Buddha speaks of the importance of knowing what is worthy of attention and what is not. In an age of information overload and AI-generated noise, this discernment is the ultimate competitive advantage. By letting go of the need for absolute certainty, you create space for a more responsive, intuitive, and compassionate leadership. You cease to be a puppet of the market's volatility and become a master of your own mental environment.
